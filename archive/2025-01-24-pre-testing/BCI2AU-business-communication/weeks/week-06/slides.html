What Defines a Business Crisis?

Three Essential Characteristics

1. Threat to Organization

  • Significant potential for negative consequences
  • Can damage reputation, operations, or financial performance
  • Requires immediate attention from leadership

2. Surprise Element

  • Often unexpected or unforeseen
  • Limited time for decision-making
  • Normal communication channels may be insufficient

3. Short Decision Time

  • Requires rapid response (hours, not days)
  • "Golden hour" concept: First 60 minutes are critical
  • Social media has compressed timelines even further

Institute for Public Relations. (2024). Crisis management and communications. https://instituteforpr.org/crisis-management-and-communications/

  • Short time: The "golden hour" used to be 60 minutes; now it's often 15 minutes or less

Framework: Argenti's Crisis Communication Principles

Five Core Principles for Crisis Response

Principle 1: Get on the Scene

Leaders must be visibly present and accessible during crises. Employees and stakeholders need tangible proof that management prioritizes their concerns (Argenti, 2002).

Principle 2: Choose Channels Carefully

Leverage multiple communication channels: voice mail, websites, social media, toll-free hotlines, media outlets. Channel redundancy ensures message saturation (Argenti, 2002).

Principle 3: Communicate with Employees First

Internal communication must precede external statements. Organizations face "a human crisis" requiring focus on people before customers or investors (Argenti, 2002).

Principle 4: Have a Plan in Place

Establish contingency work sites, backup communication infrastructure, and designated crisis communicators. Centralize decision-making while decentralizing operations (Argenti, 2002).

Principle 5: Improvise from Strong Foundations

Employees execute improvised responses effectively when company values are pre-established. Culture guides decisions when scripts fail (Argenti, 2002).

  • Principle 2 (Channels): Don't rely on one channel; what if your website crashes?
  • Principle 3 (Employees First): Internal stakeholders become your ambassadors or your critics
  • Principle 4 (Planning): The time to prepare is before the crisis, not during

Three Pillars of Effective Crisis Response

Speed: Respond within 15-30 minutes with holding statement. 74% of people expect response within one hour (American Red Cross, 2012).

Accuracy: Share only verified information. Update as facts emerge. Speed should never compromise accuracy (crisis communication research).

Empathy: Show you care about those affected. Research shows empathy is as critical as speed in crisis response (Prevency, 2024).

Formula for Success:

Fast acknowledgment + Honest communication + Genuine concern = Trust preservation

Key Teaching Points:

  • Empathy: Demonstrates care, preserves relationships

Identifying Crisis Stakeholders

Internal Stakeholders:

  • Employees (all levels - they're your frontline ambassadors)
  • Management/Leadership (decision-makers and visible representatives)
  • Board of Directors (governance and oversight responsibilities)

External Stakeholders:

  • Customers (current and potential - your revenue source)
  • Media (traditional press, bloggers, influencers - shape public narrative)
  • Investors/Shareholders (financial stakeholders concerned about impact)
  • Government/Regulators (compliance and legal obligations)
  • Community/Public (broader society, activist groups)
  • Partners/Suppliers (business continuity implications)

Priority Rule: Communicate with internal stakeholders FIRST, before external announcements (Argenti, 2002).

Internal Crisis Communication: What Employees Need

Four Essential Messages for Employees:

1. Current State of Business

  • Candid, frequent updates (at least daily) about business status
  • Employees need to know the company's actual condition as soon as reasonably possible
  • If you don't provide updates, they'll fill the information vacuum with assumptions (Leschke-Kahle, 2020)

2. Personal Impact

  • How this crisis affects their job, their team, their safety
  • What's expected of them during the crisis response
  • What support is available to them

3. Leadership Visibility

  • Hear from both top organizational leaders AND direct team leaders
  • Authentic voice matters more than polished messages (Leschke-Kahle, 2020)
  • Seeing you care matters more than perfect communication

4. Meaningful Work

  • How they can contribute to the response
  • Purpose-driven tasks restore routine and reinforce company pride (Argenti, 2002)

Leschke-Kahle, A. (2020, April 9). What employees need to hear from leaders in times of crisis. MIT Sloan Management Review. https://sloanreview.mit.edu/article/what-employees-need-to-hear-from-leaders-in-times-of-crisis/

External Crisis Communication: Customers, Media, Public

Five Essential Elements

1. Acknowledgment

  • Immediate recognition that the situation exists
  • Don't wait for complete information to acknowledge

2. Empathy

  • Express genuine concern for those affected
  • "We understand the impact this has on you..."

3. Action

  • What you're doing RIGHT NOW to address the situation
  • Concrete, specific steps (not vague promises)

4. Accountability

  • Who is responsible for fixing this
  • Transparency about what went wrong (when appropriate)

5. Timeline

  • When they can expect updates and resolution
  • Commit to specific timeframes and honor them

Channel Selection: Use platforms where your stakeholders are most active - social media for customers, press releases for media, investor calls for shareholders.

Managing Crises on Social Media

Social Media Realities:

Speed Expectation: 69% think agencies should monitor social media to offer quick help; 74% expect response within one hour (American Red Cross, 2012)

Detection Advantage: Detecting a brewing crisis 30 minutes faster can stop a rumor before it goes viral

Channel Characteristics:

  • Facebook/Zalo: Most-used platforms in Vietnam - priority for consumer crises
  • LinkedIn: Professional stakeholders - B2B or employment-related crises
  • Twitter: Real-time updates, media monitoring (limited Vietnam reach)
  • Company Website: Authoritative source for detailed information

Best Practices:

  1. Monitor social media 24/7 during crisis
  2. Respond publicly AND privately (DMs for individual concerns)
  3. Don't delete negative comments (shows you're hiding)
  4. Use consistent messaging across all platforms
  5. Provide social media team clear talking points

Research: Vietnamese organizations often post on official websites but forget social media channels, making messages less relatable to stakeholders (EloQ, 2021).

Vietnamese Context: Crisis Communication Culture

Cultural Factors in Vietnamese Crisis Response:

1. Apology Avoidance

Research shows apology is the least-used strategy among Vietnamese organizations. Companies tend to ignore crises hoping they will fade away, but social media prevents this approach from working (EloQ, 2021).

2. Hierarchy and Face

Vietnamese business culture values hierarchy and "saving face." Crisis communication must balance transparency with respect for leadership dignity. Publicly blaming leaders damages organizational cohesion.

3. Government Relations

Vietnamese companies must consider regulatory authorities as primary stakeholders in many crises (data privacy, labor issues, product safety).

4. Social Media Dominance

77% social media penetration means Vietnamese consumers expect real-time, informal communication on Facebook and Zalo - not just official press releases.

5. Community and Reputation

Collective culture means reputation damage spreads through networks quickly. Word-of-mouth (digital and traditional) can make or break crisis response.

Post-Crisis Analysis: Learning from the Experience

Four-Step Process

  • Create detailed timeline of crisis events
  • Document all communication sent (internal and external)
  • Gather stakeholder feedback and media coverage
  • Record decisions made and rationale
  • Survey employees: What did they need that they didn't get?
  • Interview key stakeholders: How did they perceive our response?
  • Analyze social media sentiment and customer feedback
  • Review media coverage tone and accuracy
  • What worked well in our crisis response?
  • What failed or fell short of expectations?
  • Were our crisis communication plans adequate?
  • Did we have the right people, tools, and authority?
  • Update crisis communication plans based on lessons learned
  • Implement preventive measures to avoid recurrence
  • Train team members on new protocols
  • Communicate improvements to stakeholders

Speaker Notes

Opening:

  • "Most organizations celebrate when the crisis ends and move on"
  • "The best organizations systematically learn from every crisis"

Key Teaching Points:

  • Documentation: Memory fades quickly, write everything down within 48 hours
  • Stakeholder feedback: Ask what they needed - don't assume you know
  • Process evaluation: Separate what you can control (plans, training) from what you can't (the crisis itself)
  • Prevention: Some crises are preventable, but all responses can improve
  • This is exactly what Vietnam's disaster management authority does after natural disasters (JICA, 2024)

Examples/Stories:

  • Vietnam example: Tech company after server outage
    • Documented: Crisis began at 2:47 PM, first acknowledgment 3:15 PM (28 min delay - TOO SLOW)
    • Learned: Need 24/7 social media monitoring, not just business hours
    • Implemented: Hired night shift social media manager
  • Positive: After food safety scare, restaurant chain implemented real-time kitchen monitoring with customer-facing cameras
  • ASEAN example: Airlines after service disruptions create "crisis playbooks" for specific scenarios

Common Misconceptions:

  • "Post-crisis analysis is blaming people" → No, it's improving systems
  • "We did fine, no need to review" → Every crisis teaches something, even successful responses

Check for Understanding:

  • "When should you document the crisis timeline?" [Within 24-48 hours while memory is fresh]
  • "Who should you get feedback from?" [All stakeholder groups - employees, customers, media, anyone affected]

Closing the Loop with Stakeholders

What to Communicate After Crisis Resolves:

To Employees:

  • Acknowledgment of their contribution during crisis
  • What was learned and what will change
  • How this experience makes the organization stronger
  • Appreciation for their resilience and adaptability

To Customers:

  • Confirmation that issue is fully resolved
  • Explanation of what caused the crisis (appropriate level of detail)
  • Specific actions taken to prevent recurrence
  • Compensation or goodwill gesture if appropriate

To Media/Public:

  • Transparent post-crisis report summarizing events
  • Organizational changes or improvements implemented
  • Reaffirmation of commitment to stakeholders
  • Availability for follow-up questions

To Government/Regulators:

  • Formal incident report with findings
  • Compliance with any regulatory requirements
  • Preventive measures implemented
  • Ongoing monitoring plan

Timeline: Post-crisis communication should occur within 1-2 weeks of resolution (while still relevant) but after immediate urgency passes.

Speaker Notes

Opening:

  • "After the crisis ends, you need to 'close the loop' with stakeholders"
  • "This is your opportunity to restore trust and demonstrate learning"

Key Teaching Points:

  • Don't just disappear when crisis ends - stakeholders want closure
  • Different stakeholders need different levels of detail
  • Timing matters: Too soon (looks defensive), too late (they've moved on)
  • This is where you shift from "crisis response" to "trust rebuilding"

Examples/Stories:

  • Vietnam example: E-commerce platform after outage
    • Email to customers: "Here's what happened, here's how we fixed it, here's your 10% discount for the inconvenience"
    • Internal memo: "Thank you for working through the weekend. Here's what we learned and what we're changing."
  • Good example: Company publishes transparent incident report on blog
  • Bad example: Company goes silent after crisis ends → Customers assume they're hiding something
  • ASEAN example: Financial services company after data breach - published detailed security improvement plan

Common Misconceptions:

  • "Bringing it up again reminds people of the crisis" → No, closure builds trust
  • "We don't owe them an explanation" → Yes you do - stakeholders deserve to know what you learned

Check for Understanding:

  • "What's the risk of NOT communicating after crisis ends?" [Stakeholders assume you didn't learn anything, trust isn't rebuilt]
  • "Should post-crisis communication be different for employees vs. customers?" [Yes - employees deserve more detail and appreciation]

Framework: Building Crisis Readiness

Four Pillars of Crisis Preparedness

Pillar 1: Crisis Communication Plan

Documented protocols for who communicates what, through which channels, to which stakeholders. Include contact lists, holding statement templates, channel access credentials.

Pillar 2: Designated Crisis Team

Identified team members with defined roles: spokesperson, social media manager, internal communicator, stakeholder liaison. Train them quarterly.

Pillar 3: Monitoring Systems

24/7 social media monitoring, media alerts, customer feedback channels, employee reporting mechanisms. Early detection prevents escalation.

Pillar 4: Scenario Planning

Pre-developed response plans for likely crisis scenarios (e.g., data breach, product recall, workplace accident, social media backlash). Practice with simulation exercises.

Testing Requirement: Crisis plans must be tested annually through tabletop exercises or simulations. Plans that aren't practiced will fail under pressure.

Speaker Notes

Opening:

  • "The best crisis response begins before the crisis happens"
  • "Argenti's principle 4: Have a plan in place - let's break down what that means"

Key Teaching Points:

  • Crisis plan: Must be documented, accessible, and up-to-date (not gathering dust in a drawer)
  • Crisis team: Can't assign roles during crisis - decide in advance, train regularly
  • Monitoring: Vietnamese companies often lack 24/7 monitoring - crisis can start at 2 AM
  • Scenario planning: Can't plan for every crisis, but can plan for categories (tech, HR, product, etc.)
  • Testing: Plans look good on paper but fail in practice without rehearsal

Examples/Stories:

  • Vietnam example: Tech startup holds quarterly "crisis simulation day"
    • Scenario: Simulated data breach at 9 AM Friday
    • Team practices entire response: holding statement, employee communication, customer notification
    • Finds gaps: Social media manager doesn't have login credentials, legal team takes too long to approve messages
  • ASEAN example: Singapore Airlines conducts crisis drills for various scenarios
  • Bad example: Company has 50-page crisis plan that no one has read or practiced

Common Misconceptions:

  • "We're too small to need a crisis plan" → Size doesn't matter, crises happen to all organizations
  • "Crisis planning is just for big disasters" → No, includes social media backlash, employee incidents, etc.

Check for Understanding:

  • "What's the minimum crisis team for a small company?" [Minimum 3: someone for internal communication, someone for external, someone for social media]
  • "How often should you test your crisis plan?" [At least annually, ideally quarterly]

Case Study: Café Disability Discrimination Incident

The Crisis:

A Vietnamese café franchise was accused of discriminating against a disabled person through a Facebook post. The public outrage grew rapidly on social media.

What Happened:

  • Accusation posted on Facebook → Viral spread within hours
  • Company initially ignored the situation (traditional Vietnamese approach)
  • Public anger intensified as company remained silent
  • Victim updated situation on his personal Facebook account
  • Only after victim's update did public sentiment cool down

Analysis Using Today's Frameworks:

Speed: Company was too slow (Argenti's Principle 2: Choose channels) ✗

Accuracy: Company didn't establish facts or share their perspective ✗

Empathy: No acknowledgment of victim's experience ✗

Stakeholder Adaptation: Ignored social media channel where crisis emerged ✗

Cultural Context: Applied old "ignore it" strategy that no longer works ✗

What Should Have Happened:

  1. Immediate (30 min): Holding statement on Facebook: "We're aware of this situation and investigating immediately. Inclusion and respect are our core values."
  2. Short-term (3 hours): Update with facts and action: "We've spoken with all involved. Here's what happened and here's how we're addressing it."
  3. Follow-up: Policy changes to prevent recurrence, staff training on accessibility

Source: EloQ Communications. (2021). Using social media during communication crisis in Vietnam. https://blog.eloqasia.com/2021/06/17/using-social-media-during-communication-crisis-in-vietnam/

Speaker Notes

Opening:

  • "Let's analyze a real Vietnamese crisis using today's frameworks"
  • "This case shows exactly what NOT to do - and why traditional approaches fail"

Key Teaching Points:

  • Speed failure: Ignoring crisis allowed it to escalate unchecked
  • Channel failure: Crisis was on Facebook, but company didn't use Facebook to respond
  • Traditional Vietnamese approach (ignore, hope it fades) documented by research as common but ineffective
  • The victim had to solve it himself by posting update - company lost control of narrative
  • This violated every principle: speed, accuracy, empathy, stakeholder adaptation

Examples/Stories:

  • Connect to today's frameworks:
    • Argenti: Get on the scene → Company was invisible ✗
    • Speed vs. Accuracy: Too slow, never even tried ✗
    • Three Pillars: No speed, no accuracy, no empathy ✗
    • Stakeholder Adaptation: Wrong channel (or no channel) ✗
  • Contrast with what SHOULD have happened (tiered response)
  • Similar Vietnamese cases: Food safety incidents where silence made it worse

Cultural Considerations:

  • Vietnamese students might think ignoring is "polite" or avoids confrontation
  • Emphasize: Modern Vietnamese consumers, especially young people, expect transparency
  • "Saving face" by ignoring crisis actually LOSES more face than honest response

  • "Which stakeholder group did they ignore?" [Customers on social media - their primary audience]

Five Critical Crisis Communication Lessons

1. Speed + Accuracy + Empathy (The Three Pillars)

Respond within 15-30 minutes with holding statement. Share only verified information. Show genuine care for those affected. All three are required for effective crisis response.

2. Employees First, Then Externals

Internal communication precedes external announcements. Employees become your ambassadors or critics based on how you treat them (Argenti, 2002).

3. Tiered Response Solves Speed vs. Accuracy

Immediate: Acknowledge (5-15 min). Short-term: Initial findings (1-3 hours). Medium-term: Detailed response (24-48 hours). Long-term: Post-crisis analysis (1-2 weeks).

4. Adapt Messages to Stakeholder Needs

Employees need business status and personal impact. Customers need acknowledgment and action. Government needs compliance. Media needs facts. One message doesn't fit all.

5. Social Media Is Primary Channel in Vietnam

77% social media penetration means Facebook and Zalo are crisis battlegrounds, not optional channels. Vietnamese consumers expect real-time, informal updates.
