BLUF: Bottom Line Up Front

Definition: State your main point, request, or conclusion in the first sentence or paragraph.

Origin: U.S. military communication standard designed to maximize mission success through precision (Sehgal, 2016).

Why it works:

  • Respects reader's time by answering "How does this affect me?" immediately
  • Reduces follow-up questions by 30-40% (military research)
  • Enables faster decision-making
  • Ensures key message received even if email only partially read

The BLUF Formula: Answer the 5 W's (Who, What, When, Where, Why) in opening.

Sehgal, K. (2016, November 22). How to write email with military precision. Harvard Business Review. https://hbr.org/2016/11/how-to-write-email-with-military-precision

Speaker Notes

Opening:

  • "Both Army Green Berets and Marine Corps fighter pilots are taught BLUF. Why? Lives depend on clear communication."
  • In business, money and careers depend on it - same principle

Key Teaching Points:

  • BLUF forces YOU to clarify your thinking before writing
  • Most people bury main point in paragraph 3-4 - reader has stopped reading by then
  • Military found "most significant problem was failing to convey focused message quickly" (Sehgal, 2016)
  • Write on board: "BLUF = Who + What + When + Where + Why in FIRST sentence"

Examples/Stories:

  • Bad example: "I hope this email finds you well. I wanted to touch base regarding the meeting we had last Tuesday where we discussed several important topics including the budget review and timeline adjustments. After careful consideration and consultation with the team..." [Students frustrated just hearing this!]
  • Good example: "Please approve the $50,000 marketing budget by Friday, March 15 so we can launch the campaign on schedule."

Common Misconceptions:

  • Students think BLUF sounds rude or too direct → Clarify: It's respectful of reader's time, still uses polite language
  • BLUF doesn't mean ONLY one sentence - it means main point FIRST, then details

Check for Understanding:

  • "I'll read two email openings. Which uses BLUF?"
  • Option A: "Thank you for your email. I have been thinking about your question and consulted with several colleagues..."
  • Option B: "I recommend we postpone the launch until April 15. Here's why..."
  • Expected: B is BLUF

Military Email Framework (Sehgal, 2016)

Component 1: Subject Line Keyword

Use capitalized tags: ACTION (requires response), INFO (FYI only), REQUEST (seeks approval), DECISION (needs judgment), SIGN (signature required), COORD (coordination needed)

Component 2: BLUF Opening

First sentence/paragraph answers: Who, What, When, Where, Why, How does this affect you?

Component 3: Background (if needed)

Use bullet points for supporting details, keep to one screen/pane without scrolling

Component 4: Active Voice

"Lan completed the report" not "The report was completed by Lan"

Component 5: Clear Call-to-Action

State exactly what you need recipient to do and by when

Sehgal, K. (2016, November 22). How to write email with military precision. Harvard Business Review. https://hbr.org/2016/11/how-to-write-email-with-military-precision

Subject Lines That Get Opened & Acted Upon

Effective subject line formula:

[KEYWORD] + [Topic] + [Deadline/Date]

Examples:

  • ✓ "ACTION: Submit budget revisions by March 15"
  • ✓ "INFO: Office closed for Tet Holiday Feb 8-12"
  • ✓ "REQUEST: Approve attached proposal - need response by Friday"
  • ✓ "DECISION: Choose vendor for website redesign by March 20"

Avoid:

  • ❌ Vague: "Quick question" "Update" "Meeting"
  • ❌ ALL CAPS EVERYTHING (looks like spam)
  • ❌ Too long: More than 60 characters gets cut off on mobile
  • ❌ No context: "Hi" "Following up" "Checking in"

Vietnamese context: Email subject lines should be more formal than Zalo messages - include project name or reference number if applicable.

  • Including deadlines in subject line increases response rate by 20-30%
  • Write subject line AFTER writing email body - then you know the true main point

Examples/Stories:

  • Bad subject line example: Student emails professor with subject "Question" - professor receives 50+ emails/day with that subject

Understanding Tone in Business Writing

Tone = The attitude or emotion conveyed through word choice, sentence structure, and punctuation.

In face-to-face: Tone communicated through voice (pitch, volume), facial expressions, body language (55% of message)

In written communication: Tone communicated ONLY through:

  • Word choice (formal vs. casual, positive vs. negative)
  • Sentence structure (short/blunt vs. longer/softer)
  • Punctuation (period vs. exclamation mark vs. question mark)
  • Formatting (bold, ALL CAPS, italics)

The challenge: Without vocal and visual cues, written tone is easily misinterpreted. What you intend as "efficient" may read as "rude."

Speaker Notes

Opening:

  • "Raise your hand if you've ever sent a text message that was misunderstood"
  • Same problem in professional email - but with higher stakes

Key Teaching Points:

  • Research shows we overestimate how well our tone comes across in writing by 50%
  • You THINK you sound friendly, reader perceives neutral or cold
  • Brain processes emotional connotations of words within 200 milliseconds - faster than conscious meaning (Birchard, 2021)
  • This means tone hits reader before they even fully understand your message

Examples/Stories:

  • Classic example: "Fine." (with period) vs. "Fine!" (with exclamation)
  • Same word, completely different tone based on punctuation alone
  • Vietnamese student example: Writing "Please do this" - intended as polite request, read as command by Western colleague

Common Misconceptions:

  • Students think emoticons/emojis solve tone problem → Appropriate in Zalo/Teams, NOT in formal email to clients or superiors
  • Some think ALL CAPS emphasizes importance → Actually signals shouting/anger in digital communication

Check for Understanding:

  • "How would you interpret this email: 'We need to talk about your report.'"
  • Expected responses: Negative, worried, something's wrong
  • "How about: 'I'd love to discuss your excellent report - you raised great points.'"
  • Expected: Positive, encouraging

The Professional Tone Spectrum

Formal (High Stakes)

First contact with senior executives, clients, external stakeholders, bad news, sensitive topics

Example: "Dear Mr. Nguyen, Thank you for your inquiry regarding the project timeline. I am writing to provide a detailed update and address your questions."

Professional (Standard Business)

Colleagues at same level, routine updates, project coordination, most daily email

Example: "Hi Minh, Here's the update on the March timeline. Let me know if you have questions."

Casual (Internal/Familiar)

Close team members, quick questions, ongoing conversations with established rapport

Example: "Hey team - quick reminder about Friday's deadline. Thanks!"

Key Principle: When in doubt, err on the side of more formal. You can always become more casual later as relationship develops.

Speaker Notes

Opening:

  • "There's no single 'correct' tone - only appropriate tone for context, relationship, and culture"
  • Draw spectrum on board from Formal → Professional → Casual

Key Teaching Points:

  • First email to someone = always more formal (you can't take back too-casual first impression)
  • Match recipient's tone level in replies (if they're formal, stay formal; if casual, you can shift casual)
  • High-stakes situations (complaints, bad news, disagreements) require more formal tone
  • Vietnamese business culture generally more formal than U.S./Australia, less formal than Japan/Korea

Examples/Stories:

  • Formal example: Email to company CEO or government official - use full titles, complete sentences, formal closings
  • Professional example: Email to project manager you work with weekly - friendly but businesslike
  • Casual example: Slack message to teammate sitting next to you - conversational, abbreviations OK
  • Vietnamese context: Even in casual Zalo messages with colleagues, common to start with "Anh/Chị" honorific

Cultural Considerations:

  • Vietnamese communication often more indirect than Western - "I will try" might mean "probably not" rather than actual commitment
  • When working with international teams, Vietnamese students should be slightly MORE direct than feels natural
  • Hierarchy matters: Email to boss requires more formal tone than email to peer, even about same topic

  • Younger generations can always be casual → Many young professionals prefer professional tone to be taken seriously
  • Formal = longer → No, formal is about word choice and structure, not length (BLUF still applies)

The Eight S's Framework (Birchard, 2021)

Evidence-based principles from neuroscience research

1. **Simplicity** - Short sentences and familiar vocabulary increase "processing fluency" 2. **Specificity** - Concrete words activate more brain regions than abstract terms 3. **Surprise** - Unexpected information 14% more likely to be shared and remembered 4. **Stirring language** - Emotional words bypass cognitive processing for faster impact 5. **Seductiveness** - Anticipation activates brain's reward circuits 6. **Smart ideas** - "Aha" moments light up reward regions, leaving readers satisfied 7. **Social content** - Human-centered writing activates social cognition areas 8. **Storytelling** - Narratives synchronize brain patterns between writer and reader

Bottom line: Writing that aligns with how brains process information is more persuasive, memorable, and effective.

Simplicity: How Brains Process Text

Research findings (Birchard, 2021)

  • **Passive voice** reduces comprehension accuracy by 10% and slows reading speed
  • **Long sentences** (30+ words) require re-reading 40% more often
  • **Jargon/complex words** activate fewer neural pathways than familiar terms

Practical applications for email:

  • Active voice: "I completed the analysis" (not "The analysis was completed")
  • Short sentences: Average 15-20 words (occasionally vary for rhythm)
  • Familiar words: "use" not "utilize," "help" not "facilitate"
  • One idea per sentence: Don't combine multiple thoughts

Result: Reader understands faster, remembers longer, acts sooner.

Speaker Notes

Opening:

  • "Your brain is lazy - it prefers easy-to-process information"
  • This isn't about dumbing down; it's about respecting cognitive limits

Key Teaching Points:

  • Processing fluency affects memory: Easy-to-read text remembered 30% better after 24 hours
  • Active voice not just clearer - it's literally faster for brain to process
  • Military email format (Sehgal) and neuroscience research (Birchard) reach same conclusion: Simplicity wins
  • Vietnamese students learning English sometimes use overly complex vocabulary to sound professional - simple is actually more impressive

Examples/Stories:

  • Complex: "The utilization of synergistic approaches will facilitate the optimization of outcomes."
  • Simple: "Working together will improve results."
  • Same meaning, second version activates more neural pathways and is remembered better
  • Vietnamese example: "We will implement the initiative" → "We will start the project" (clearer for international teams)

Common Misconceptions:

  • Simple language sounds unprofessional → Actually, leaders like Jeff Bezos and Warren Buffett famous for simple, clear writing
  • Longer sentences show sophistication → Brain science shows opposite - harder to process, easier to forget

Check for Understanding:

Specificity: Why Concrete Words Activate More Brain Regions

Research finding: Concrete words (like "pelican") activate more neurons in visual and motor brain regions than abstract words (like "bird"), causing "the brain to process meaning more robustly" (Birchard, 2021).

In business writing:

❌ Abstract/Vague

  • "We need to improve customer satisfaction soon."
  • "Sales increased significantly."
  • "Please update me on progress."
  • Specific/Concrete
  • "We need to reduce response time from 24 hours to 4 hours by March 31."
  • "Sales increased 23% in Q1, from $2.1M to $2.6M."
  • "Please send me the completed vendor comparison spreadsheet by Friday 5pm."

Benefits of specificity:

  • Activates more neural pathways = better comprehension and memory
  • Eliminates ambiguity and need for follow-up clarification
  • Enables action (recipient knows exactly what to do)

Speaker Notes

Opening:

  • "Your brain creates mental images when reading concrete words - this deepens processing"
  • "Bird" is abstract; "pelican" creates specific visual image that sticks in memory

Key Teaching Points:

  • Specificity critical for BLUF technique - vague main point defeats the purpose
  • Numbers, dates, deadlines = concrete; "soon," "later," "a lot" = abstract and useless
  • Brain scans show concrete language lights up visual cortex even when just reading silently
  • This is why stories and examples more memorable than abstract principles

Examples/Stories:

  • Vague email: "Please finish the report quickly" - What's "quickly"? Today? This week?
  • Specific email: "Please submit the Q1 sales report by Thursday, March 14 at 5pm"
  • Vietnamese context: Vietnamese time orientation sometimes more flexible (less clock-focused) - when working with international teams, specify exact dates/times to avoid misunderstanding
  • "This week" to Western colleague might mean Monday-Friday; to Vietnamese colleague might include weekend

Cultural Considerations:

  • Vietnamese communication sometimes uses indirect language to maintain harmony - "maybe we could consider possibly..."
  • In business email, especially with international partners, be more specific and direct
  • Replace "I will try" with "I will complete by [date]" or "I cannot complete by [date] but can by [alternate date]"

Common Misconceptions:

  • Specificity takes longer → Actually saves time by eliminating back-and-forth clarification
  • Being specific sounds demanding → Specificity shows respect for everyone's time

Check for Understanding:

Social Content: Why "You" Matters

Research finding: Human-centered writing activates brain regions that interpret social signals. Readers infer character traits within 650 milliseconds of reading (Birchard, 2021).

Second-person pronouns ("you") significantly improve learning retention - studies show material written with "you" remembered better than third-person text.

In business email:

❌ Impersonal/Passive

  • "The report should be submitted by Friday."
  • "It has been decided that the meeting will be postponed."
  • "This project requires additional resources."
  • Human-Centered/Active
  • "Could you please submit the report by Friday?"
  • "I've decided to postpone our meeting to give you more prep time."
  • "You'll need two additional team members to meet the project deadline."

Why it works: Activates social cognition - brain processes the email as human interaction, not just information transfer.

Speaker Notes

Opening:

  • "Email is social interaction, not just information transmission"
  • Brain treats reading email from person same way it processes face-to-face conversation

Key Teaching Points:

  • Using "you" and "I" makes writing feel personal even in professional context
  • Brain scans show social cognition areas light up when reading human-centered language
  • This connects to strategic communication framework from Week 1: know-feel-do requires understanding your AUDIENCE (the "you")
  • Vietnamese students sometimes taught to avoid "I" (sounds self-centered) - in English business writing, "I" shows ownership and accountability

Examples/Stories:

  • Passive/impersonal: "It has been determined that..." - Who determined? No human agent visible
  • Active/personal: "I recommend we..." or "The leadership team decided..." - Clear human ownership
  • Story: Amazon banned PowerPoint presentations in favor of 6-page narrative memos - Jeff Bezos wanted human voice and clear thinking, not bullet points
  • Vietnamese context: Using "you" in English email = professional and direct; using "you" in Vietnamese ("em," "anh," "chị") requires understanding hierarchy and relationship

Cultural Considerations:

  • English business writing values directness and personal accountability ("I will..." "You need to...")
  • Vietnamese communication often uses passive constructions to avoid assigning blame or sounding too direct
  • When writing to international colleagues, embrace "I" and "you" - it shows confidence and clarity

Common Misconceptions:

  • Professional writing should be impersonal → Research shows opposite - human-centered writing more effective
  • Using "I" sounds arrogant → Actually, taking ownership (good or bad) shows leadership

Check for Understanding:

  • "Make this more human-centered: 'The deadline has been missed and the project is now delayed.'"
  • Good answers: "We missed the deadline, and I've pushed the project completion to March 30." or "You mentioned you couldn't meet Friday's deadline - what's a realistic new target?"

Professional Email Pitfalls

❌ TOP 10 EMAIL MISTAKES:

  1. Reply-all abuse - Only use when ALL recipients need your response
  2. Unclear subject lines - "Quick question" or "Update" tells recipient nothing
  3. No greeting/closing - Comes across as curt even if unintended
  4. Excessive exclamation marks!!! - Sounds overeager or unprofessional
  5. Writing angry - Wait 24 hours before sending heated email; tone impossible to soften later
  6. Burying the main point - Violates BLUF; recipient stops reading
  7. No proofreading - Typos undermine credibility
  8. Forwarding without context - "FYI" with long email thread - summarize key points
  9. Emailing after hours - In Vietnam, sends message that work-life balance unimportant
  10. No call-to-action - Recipient unsure what you need from them

Remember: Email creates permanent record. Write every email assuming it could be forwarded to anyone.

Speaker Notes

Opening:

  • "Everyone makes these mistakes - awareness is first step to avoiding them"
  • "Which of these have YOU done or received?" [Show of hands - builds rapport]

Key Teaching Points:

  • Reply-all particularly problematic in large organizations - sends unnecessary emails to dozens of people
  • Exclamation marks: One per email maximum (use for genuine enthusiasm only)
  • Writing angry: Neuroscience shows emotional regulation takes 6+ hours - write draft, save, review tomorrow before sending
  • Vietnamese cultural note: Emailing after hours (late night, weekends) viewed as disrespectful of personal time - unlike US "hustle culture"
  • Proofreading critical for non-native English speakers - ask colleague to review important emails before sending

Examples/Stories:

  • Real consequence: Employee sent angry email to manager late Friday night → Read by entire leadership team by Monday morning (manager forwarded) → Employee reputation damaged for months
  • Vietnamese context: Student forwarded professor's email containing feedback about another student → Violated trust and privacy, damaged all relationships
  • Zalo vs. email cultural difference: After-hours Zalo messages more accepted in Vietnam, but email should respect boundaries

Cultural Considerations:

  • Vietnamese communication often indirect - may say "maybe" when they mean "no" to avoid conflict
  • When working with international teams, clearer to say "I cannot do this because..." than to imply through vague language
  • "Face" concept: Never send email that could embarrass recipient publicly - handle sensitive issues privately

Common Misconceptions:

  • Shorter emails always better → No, emails should be as long as needed to be CLEAR, but no longer
  • Can use emoji/Zalo-style language in work email → Absolutely not for external or formal internal emails
  • Email permanently stored → Actually true! Assume every email could be read in court or by CEO

Check for Understanding:

  • Expected: Write draft, save, review tomorrow when calm, soften tone before sending (or better, have face-to-face conversation)

Three Key Takeaways

1. Structure for Clarity (Military Precision)

  • BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front): Main point in first sentence
  • Keyword subject lines: ACTION, REQUEST, INFO, DECISION
  • One-pane format: Fit content without scrolling
  • Active voice and bullets for background

2. Tone for Relationship (Strategic Adaptation)

  • Match tone to audience, context, and culture
  • Professional tone = problem-solving, not blame
  • Use "you" and "I" for human connection
  • When in doubt, err on side of more formal

3. Neuroscience for Impact (Evidence-Based Writing)

  • Simplicity: Short sentences, familiar words, active voice
  • Specificity: Concrete details, numbers, dates, deadlines
  • Social content: Human-centered language activates brain's reward circuits

Bottom Line: Email mastery = Strategic thinking + Tactical execution + Cultural awareness
