# Week 3 Tutorial: Tutor Notes

**Course:** Business Communication (BCI2AU)
**Tutorial:** Persuasive Communication & Influence
**Duration:** 90 minutes

---

## Tutorial Goals

- Prepare students for the Persuasive Proposal component of their Written Portfolio (due Week 5, 10% of grade)
- Provide hands-on practice applying Cialdini's six principles and problem-solution-benefit framework
- Build familiarity with rubric criteria through peer review before graded submission

## Key Outcomes

By the end of this tutorial, students should be able to:
- Structure a persuasive business proposal using problem-solution-benefit framework
- Explicitly identify and apply at least 2-3 Cialdini principles in a proposal
- Use rubric criteria to evaluate persuasive writing (their own and peers')
- Distinguish ethical persuasion from manipulation in business contexts
- Demonstrate readiness for Quiz 1 (Week 4) through practice question performance

## Quiz Solutions

### Question 1: Cialdini Principles - Definitions
**Correct: B) Social Proof**

**Why B is correct:**
Social proof is defined as people's tendency to follow what others are doing, especially when uncertain. This principle explains why customer reviews, testimonials, and "bestseller" labels are persuasive - we look to others' behavior as a guide for our own decisions.

**Why wrong:**
- **A) Authority:** This is about deferring to experts or credible sources, not following peer behavior
- **C) Liking:** This principle is about saying yes to people we like (similarity, compliments, cooperation)
- **D) Commitment & Consistency:** This is about wanting to be consistent with previous statements or actions, not following others

**Common confusion:** Students sometimes confuse social proof with authority. Authority = following experts; social proof = following peers/crowd.

**Teaching tip:** Use the Vietnamese e-commerce example: "When Shopee shows '10,000 sold,' that's social proof - you're following what other customers did. When they show 'Shopee Mall Verified Seller,' that's authority - you're trusting an expert certification."

---

### Question 2: Cialdini Principles - Application
**Correct: C) Social Proof**

**Why C is correct:**
Displaying "12,847 people bought this product in the last 24 hours" is a classic application of social proof. It leverages the psychological principle that when we're uncertain about a purchase decision, we look to what others are doing. The high number of buyers signals that the product is popular and trustworthy, reducing perceived risk.

**Why wrong:**
- **A) Scarcity:** While numbers can indicate scarcity ("Only 3 left"), this example shows how many people bought it (popularity), not limited availability
- **B) Reciprocity:** This principle involves giving something first to create obligation. Displaying purchase statistics doesn't give anything to the customer
- **D) Authority:** This would involve expert endorsement or credentials, not peer behavior statistics

**Common confusion:** Students might think large numbers always indicate scarcity, but scarcity is about *limited* availability, not how many people want it.

**Teaching tip:** "Ask yourself: Is this showing what OTHERS did (social proof) or what's RUNNING OUT (scarcity)? '12,847 bought this' = others did it. 'Only 3 left' = running out."

### Question 3: Problem-Solution-Benefit Framework
**Correct: C) Benefit**

**Why C is correct:**
The Benefit section is specifically designed to quantify what the audience gains from accepting your recommendation. This section answers "What's in it for me?" from the decision-maker's perspective and should include quantified outcomes like cost savings, revenue increases, time saved, or productivity improvements.

**Why wrong:**
- **A) Problem:** This section defines the issue and creates urgency, but doesn't quantify what they gain from solving it
- **B) Solution:** This section presents your recommendation and proves it works, but doesn't focus on the audience's specific gains
- **D) Executive Summary:** While this might mention benefits, it's not one of the three core framework sections and isn't specifically focused on quantifying gains

**Common confusion:** Students sometimes put benefits in the solution section. Clarify: Solution = what you propose; Benefit = what they gain.

**Teaching tip:** "Think of it this way: Problem = 'Here's what's wrong.' Solution = 'Here's what to do.' Benefit = 'Here's what you'll get.' Only the Benefit section quantifies the payoff."

### Question 4: Ethical Persuasion vs. Manipulation
**Correct: B) Ethical persuasion is transparent and preserves choice, manipulation is hidden and coercive**

**Why B is correct:**
According to Cialdini (2013), the fundamental distinction between ethical persuasion and manipulation lies in transparency and choice. Ethical persuasion is open about its intent and respects the audience's freedom to decide. Manipulation hides its persuasive intent and uses tactics that remove or limit genuine choice (deception, pressure, guilt).

**Why wrong:**
- **A)** While ethical persuasion often uses research, this isn't the defining difference. Manipulation can also cite research (selectively or dishonestly).
- **C)** This is backwards - ethical persuasion can be highly effective. The difference isn't effectiveness, it's ethics.
- **D)** Cialdini's principles are universal across cultures. Cultural context affects how principles are applied, but the ethics distinction applies everywhere.

**Common confusion:** Students might think manipulation is just "more aggressive" persuasion. Clarify: It's about transparency and choice, not intensity.

**Teaching tip:** "Use Cialdini's test: 'Would I feel good if someone used this tactic on my family?' If it relies on hiding information or removing choice, it's manipulation."

---

### Question 5: Credibility Components
**Correct: D) Confidence**

**Why D is correct:**
Hallenbeck & Childs (2022) identify three pillars of credibility: Competence (expertise and knowledge), Trustworthiness (honesty and reliability), and Goodwill (caring about audience's interests). Confidence is not one of these pillars - while confident delivery can help, credibility is built on competence, trustworthiness, and goodwill.

**Why wrong:**
- **A) Competence:** This IS a pillar - demonstrating expertise and relevant experience
- **B) Trustworthiness:** This IS a pillar - being honest about limitations and providing balanced perspectives
- **C) Goodwill:** This IS a pillar - showing you care about the audience's interests, not just your own

**Common confusion:** Students might assume confidence is part of credibility because confident speakers seem more credible. But confidence without competence, trustworthiness, or goodwill doesn't create lasting credibility.

**Teaching tip:** "Think of a confident person who lacks expertise (incompetent), lies (untrustworthy), or only cares about themselves (no goodwill). Are they credible? No. Confidence helps delivery, but doesn't replace the three pillars."

### Question 6: Cialdini Principles - Reciprocity
**Correct: B) Reciprocity**

**Why B is correct:**
Offering a free 30-day trial is a classic application of reciprocity. The company gives something valuable first (free access to the product) with the expectation that users will feel obligated to reciprocate by purchasing. The psychological principle is that when someone gives us something, we feel a natural obligation to give back.

**Why wrong:**
- **A) Scarcity:** This would involve limited-time offers or limited availability, not free trials
- **C) Commitment & Consistency:** While using the product creates some commitment, the primary principle at work is reciprocity (giving before asking)
- **D) Social Proof:** This would involve showing how many others use the product, not giving free access

**Common confusion:** Students might see the trial period as scarcity ("only 30 days"). Clarify: The scarcity of time creates urgency to decide, but the free value creates reciprocity.

**Teaching tip:** "Reciprocity = giving first, then asking. The company gives you 30 days free (the give), then asks you to purchase (the ask). They're creating psychological debt."

---

### Question 7: Vietnamese Business Context - Reciprocity
**Correct: B) Offering valuable insights or assistance before asking for a business favor**

**Why B is correct:**
This correctly demonstrates "có qua có lại" (reciprocity) in Vietnamese business culture. By offering value first (insights, assistance, knowledge), you create a sense of obligation that makes the other party more likely to help you later. This aligns with Vietnamese cultural values of mutual exchange and relationship-building before business transactions.

**Why wrong:**
- **A)** Aggressive follow-ups are pressure tactics that violate Vietnamese business norms around harmony and indirect communication
- **C)** High-pressure sales tactics contradict Vietnamese cultural preferences for relationship-based, low-pressure business interactions
- **D)** While scarcity is a valid persuasion principle, it's not reciprocity and doesn't demonstrate "có qua có lại"

**Common confusion:** Students from high-pressure sales environments might think aggressive tactics are persuasive. In Vietnamese culture, they damage relationships.

**Teaching tip:** "In Vietnamese business culture, you build 'quan hệ' (relationships) first through reciprocity. Give help, advice, or value before asking for anything. The business dinner before negotiations? That's reciprocity in action."

### Question 8: Persuasive Message Structure
**Correct: C) It creates urgency and buy-in before presenting your recommendation**

**Why C is correct:**
Starting with the problem creates buy-in by establishing that there IS a problem worth solving. If the audience doesn't feel the problem is urgent or important, they won't care about your solution. The problem section uses data and evidence to make the audience feel the pain or missed opportunity, which creates receptiveness to your solution.

**Why wrong:**
- **A)** Length and impressiveness aren't the goals - persuasiveness is. A short, urgent problem statement is more effective than a long, rambling one.
- **B)** While the framework is taught, the reason to use it isn't to "follow format" - it's because it's psychologically effective
- **D)** This has nothing to do with APA formatting, which is for citations, not proposal structure

**Common confusion:** Students sometimes want to jump straight to "Here's my great idea!" But without establishing the problem first, the audience has no context for why your idea matters.

**Teaching tip:** "Think about it: If I say 'We should buy new computers,' you might ask 'Why? What's wrong with our computers?' But if I first establish 'Our computers crash 3 times daily, costing 2 hours of productivity per employee,' now you WANT to hear my solution."

---

### Question 9: Building Credibility - What Decreases It
**Correct: C) Using absolute terms like "guaranteed" and "always" without evidence**

**Why C is correct:**
Using absolute terms like "guaranteed," "always," "never," or "100% certain" without solid evidence DECREASES credibility because it signals overconfidence or dishonesty. Most business outcomes have some uncertainty, so claiming guarantees makes you seem either naive or deceptive. This violates the "trustworthiness" pillar of credibility by making unprovable claims.

**Why wrong:**
- **A)** Citing specific research WITH data INCREASES credibility by demonstrating competence and providing evidence
- **B)** Acknowledging limitations honestly INCREASES credibility by building trustworthiness (you're not hiding downsides)
- **D)** Industry-specific examples INCREASE credibility by showing competence and understanding of context

**Common confusion:** Students might think strong, confident language ("guaranteed!") builds credibility. Actually, overconfident claims without proof destroy trustworthiness.

**Teaching tip:** "Compare these: 'This will definitely work' vs. 'Based on 5 similar implementations, this has an 80% success rate.' Which sounds more credible? The second one - it's confident but honest about uncertainty."

### Question 10: Authority Principle - Vietnamese Context
**Correct: B) Authority**

**Why B is correct:**
Featuring doctors in white coats is a classic application of the authority principle. The white coat is a visual signal of medical expertise and credentials, which increases persuasive power. Vietnamese culture has high power distance, meaning authority figures (doctors, professors, government officials) are especially influential. Even when the doctor isn't famous, the credentials and symbols of authority (white coat, stethoscope, hospital setting) trigger the authority principle.

**Why wrong:**
- **A) Social Proof:** This would be showing how many customers use the product or displaying reviews, not expert endorsement
- **C) Liking:** This principle is about similarity, attractiveness, or rapport, not expertise
- **D) Scarcity:** This would involve limited availability or time pressure, not expert endorsement

**Common confusion:** Students might think "lots of people trust doctors" = social proof. Clarify: We trust doctors because they're experts (authority), not because we're following what others do (social proof).

**Teaching tip:** "Ask: Why are we persuaded? If it's because they're an EXPERT (authority), that's the authority principle. If it's because MANY OTHERS bought it (social proof), that's social proof. Doctors in white coats = expert authority."

## Facilitating the Main Activity

**Context:**

The VietTech Solutions scenario is deliberately open-ended to allow multiple valid proposal approaches. Students can propose different combinations of program components (mentorship, training budgets, certifications, conferences, etc.) and all can be correct if well-reasoned. The goal is to practice applying the problem-solution-benefit framework and Cialdini principles, not to find "the right answer." This mimics real professional situations where there are multiple defensible solutions to business problems. Emphasize that reasoning and framework application matter more than which specific programs they choose.

**What Good Work Looks Like:**

- **Clear problem statement** using the $787,500 annual turnover cost and exit interview data to create urgency
- **Specific solution** choosing 3-4 program components that work together coherently (not proposing everything)
- **Quantified benefits** using the LinkedIn or Deloitte research to calculate ROI (e.g., "If this reduces turnover by 25%, we save ~$197,000 annually while investing ~$75,000 in the program = $122,000 net benefit")
- **Explicit Cialdini principles** identified by name with clear application (e.g., "I'm applying social proof by citing that 94% of employees stay longer at companies investing in development (LinkedIn, 2023)")
- **Ethical persuasion** demonstrating transparency (clear intent), truthfulness (accurate use of data), and mutual benefit (good for employees AND company)

**Critical Moments:**

**During Individual/Pair Work (25 minutes):** Around the 10-minute mark, check in with pairs to ensure they understand the task and have started drafting. Common early struggles include: trying to propose too many program components (encourage them to focus on 3-4), not quantifying the problem cost (point them back to the $787,500 figure), or forgetting to explicitly name the Cialdini principles they're using. Around minute 15-20, some pairs will finish drafting and start polishing - encourage them to check their work against the four criteria (structure, Cialdini principles, evidence, ethics) rather than just proofreading. Vietnamese students may be hesitant to make choices without "the right answer" - reassure them that multiple approaches are valid and their reasoning is what matters. If students finish very early (before 20 minutes), challenge them to strengthen quantification of benefits or add another Cialdini principle.

**During Peer Review (15 minutes):** This is where students learn the rubric by applying it. Circulate actively and listen to feedback conversations. Common issues: Vietnamese students may be too polite and only give positive feedback (gently push them to find one genuine area to strengthen), or they may focus on grammar instead of framework application (redirect to the four criteria). If you overhear a student saying "This is wrong" or "This doesn't work," intervene to reframe: "What's one way this could be strengthened while keeping their overall approach?" The goal is comparing strategies, not declaring winners and losers. Around minute 10, ensure pairs have provided written feedback and the original authors have received it - some pairs spend too long discussing and don't write anything down. Remind students that the person receiving feedback needs specific notes to revise effectively.

**During Revision & Debrief (15 minutes):** Give students 10 full minutes to revise - they should be actively editing their proposals based on feedback, not just reading feedback passively. This is when learning solidifies - they see how to strengthen framework application in real-time. During the 5-minute debrief, ask 2-3 volunteers to share one thing they improved based on feedback. If no one volunteers (common in Vietnamese classrooms due to cultural hesitance), cold-call gently: "Can someone share what feedback they received and how they strengthened their proposal?" Highlight variety: if one pair focused heavily on social proof while another focused on authority and ROI, point out that both are valid approaches demonstrating different strategic thinking. Emphasize that there's no single "right" proposal - this builds confidence for Week 5 when they'll write their graded version.

**Timing Flexibility:**

If the main activity runs long (likely, as students often need 30 minutes instead of 25 for drafting), you have flexibility with the quiz prep section. The quiz prep can be shortened to 10 minutes (5 questions instead of 10) or even assigned as homework if time is tight. Priority is ensuring students complete a full draft proposal and receive peer feedback, as this is the core learning activity. However, don't skip quiz prep entirely - students need some practice before Quiz 1 next week. If you're running 5-10 minutes over, compress debrief to 3 minutes and move quickly through quiz answers without extended discussion. The tutorial can flexibly run 85-95 minutes depending on class engagement and pace.
