**SLIDE 8: Cialdini's Six Principles - Part 1**

TITLE: Cialdini's Six Principles of Persuasion (Part 1)

CONTENT:

**1. Reciprocity (Đối ứng)**
- **Principle:** People feel obligated to return favors
- **How it works:** When you give something first, people want to give back
- **Business application:** Free samples, free trials, valuable content before asking for sale
- **Vietnamese context:** "Có qua có lại" - deeply rooted cultural value
- **Example:** Shopee's free shipping vouchers create obligation to purchase

**2. Commitment & Consistency (Cam kết & Nhất quán)**
- **Principle:** People want to be consistent with what they've previously said or done
- **How it works:** Small commitments lead to larger ones
- **Business application:** Start with small "yes" (free newsletter signup) → bigger "yes" (purchase)
- **Example:** Lazada's "Add to wishlist" → "Add to cart" → "Purchase" sequence
- **Vietnamese context:** Public commitments especially powerful in collectivist culture

(Cialdini, 2001)

[PURPOSE: Introduce first two principles with cultural context]
[TIMING: 4 minutes]

**Key Teaching Points:**

- Emphasize Vietnamese cultural resonance: "Có qua có lại" isn't just a saying, it's a cultural expectation

**SLIDE 9: Cialdini's Six Principles - Part 2**

TITLE: Cialdini's Six Principles of Persuasion (Part 2)

CONTENT:

**3. Social Proof (Bằng chứng xã hội)**
- **Principle:** People follow what others are doing, especially when uncertain
- **How it works:** We look to others' behavior as a guide for our own
- **Business application:** Customer reviews, testimonials, "bestseller" labels, user counts
- **Vietnamese context:** Extremely powerful in collectivist culture - group behavior guides individual decisions
- **Example:** Zalo's "77.7 million monthly active users" messaging - if millions use it, it must be good

**4. Authority (Thẩm quyền)**
- **Principle:** People defer to experts and credible sources
- **How it works:** Credentials, titles, expertise signals increase persuasive power
- **Business application:** Expert endorsements, credentials display, professional titles
- **Vietnamese context:** High power distance culture - authority figures especially influential
- **Example:** Vietnamese pharmaceutical ads featuring doctors in white coats to signal medical authority

(Cialdini, 2001)

**SLIDE 13: The Problem-Solution-Benefit Framework**

<!-- LAYOUT: framework -->

TITLE: The Problem-Solution-Benefit Framework

CONTENT:

The three-part structure for persuasive proposals:

**1. PROBLEM**
- Clearly define the problem or opportunity
- Use data and evidence to establish urgency
- Make the audience feel the pain or missed opportunity

**2. SOLUTION**
- Present your recommendation or proposal
- Show why this solution addresses the problem
- Provide evidence that it works (case studies, data, expert endorsement)

**3. BENEFIT**
- Explain what the audience gains (not what you gain)
- Quantify benefits when possible (cost savings, revenue increase, time saved)
- Connect to audience's goals and motivations

**Why this works:** Follows natural decision-making process: recognize problem → evaluate solutions → choose based on benefit.

[PURPOSE: Teach core persuasive structure framework]
[TIMING: 4 minutes]

---

## Speaker Notes

**Opening:**
- Write on board: "Problem → Solution → Benefit" and keep this visible
- "This is the structure for almost every persuasive proposal you'll write in your career."

**Key Teaching Points:**
- **Problem:** Don't skip this - if the audience doesn't feel the problem is urgent, they won't care about your solution
- Use the "So what?" test: After stating the problem, ask "So what? Why does this matter?" Keep answering until you hit real urgency
- **Solution:** This is where you present your recommendation, but don't just describe it - prove it works
- **Benefit:** Focus on THEIR benefit, not yours. Common mistake: "This will help me learn..." should be "This will save the company $50,000..."
- Quantify when possible: "This will improve productivity" is weak. "This will reduce processing time by 30%, saving 10 hours per week" is strong.

**Examples/Stories:**
- **Business proposal example:** "Our customer service response time is 48 hours, 20 hours slower than competitors (Problem). Implementing a chatbot for FAQs will handle 60% of inquiries instantly (Solution). This will improve customer satisfaction scores by an estimated 25% and reduce support costs by $30,000 annually (Benefit)."
- **Vietnamese context:** Vietnamese decision-makers expect thorough justification - the problem section must be well-documented
- **Weak vs. strong:** Weak: "We should use Slack." Strong: "Email threads cause 3-hour delays in project decisions (Problem). Slack enables real-time communication (Solution). Teams using Slack report 30% faster decision-making and 50% fewer missed messages (Benefit)."

**Common Misconceptions:**
- "I should start with my solution since that's what I'm proposing." → No, start with the problem to create buy-in first
- "Benefits are obvious, I don't need to spell them out." → Never assume - always explicitly state benefits

**Check for Understanding:**
- "If I'm proposing that our team adopt a new project management tool, what would my Problem section focus on?" [Current inefficiencies, missed deadlines, communication breakdowns]
- "What's the difference between a solution and a benefit?" [Solution = what you propose; Benefit = what the audience gains]

TITLE: Example Proposal: Implementing Flexible Work Policy

CONTENT:

**PROBLEM**

- 23% of employees report burnout (internal survey, Q3 2024)
- Difficulty recruiting top talent (45% of job candidates ask about flexibility)

**SLIDE 19: Ethical Persuasion vs. Manipulation**

TITLE: Ethical Persuasion vs. Manipulation

<!-- LAYOUT: comparison-table -->

CONTENT:

| **Ethical Persuasion** | **Manipulation** |
|------------------------|------------------|
| **Transparent:** Audience knows you're trying to persuade | **Hidden:** Audience unaware of persuasive intent |
| **Truthful:** Based on accurate information and honest claims | **Deceptive:** Uses false or misleading information |
| **Respectful:** Preserves audience's right to choose | **Coercive:** Removes or limits real choice |
| **Mutual benefit:** Creates value for both parties | **One-sided:** Benefits only the persuader |
| **Long-term:** Builds lasting relationships and trust | **Short-term:** Exploits for immediate gain, damages trust |
| **Informed choice:** Provides information needed to decide | **Withholds info:** Hides downsides or critical facts |

**Cialdini's test:** "Would I feel comfortable if someone used this tactic on me or my family?"

*Cialdini, R. B. (2013). The uses (and abuses) of influence. Harvard Business Review, 91(7-8), 76-81.*

[PURPOSE: Clearly distinguish ethical persuasion from manipulation]
[TIMING: 5 minutes]

---

## Speaker Notes

**Opening:**
- "Here's the fundamental distinction. Every persuasive tactic you use should pass this test: Would you be okay if someone used it on your mother?"

**Key Teaching Points:**
- **Transparency:** Ethical persuasion doesn't hide its intent. When a salesperson says "Let me tell you why this product is right for you," you know they're selling. Manipulation hides intent.
- **Truthful:** This is non-negotiable. Fake scarcity ("only 2 left!" when there are 200) is manipulation. Real scarcity is ethical persuasion.
- **Respectful of choice:** You're making it easier to say "yes," but they can still say "no" without penalty. High-pressure sales that won't let you leave = manipulation.
- **Mutual benefit:** Both parties should gain value. If only you benefit, that's exploitation.
- **Long-term thinking:** Would this persuasion tactic damage trust if discovered later? If yes, it's manipulation.

**Examples/Stories:**
- **Ethical persuasion example:** Shopee's flash sales - the sale actually ends at the stated time, the quantity is actually limited. That's ethical scarcity.
- **Manipulation example:** A company claims "As seen on TV!" but it was a 5-second mention. That's misleading authority.
- **Vietnamese context violation:** A business partner takes you to an expensive dinner (reciprocity), then immediately asks for a decision on a contract before you can review it. That's manipulative reciprocity.
- **Cialdini's personal example:** He was invited to a "free investment seminar." The seminar was real (free value). But at the end, there was hard-sell pressure to invest immediately or "lose the opportunity." The free seminar was ethical; the high-pressure close was manipulative.

**Common Misconceptions:**
- "All sales tactics are manipulative." → No, ethical persuasion is about helping people make good decisions, providing genuine value
- "If it works, it's fine." → No, short-term gains from manipulation destroy long-term trust and reputation

**Check for Understanding:**
- "Is it ethical persuasion or manipulation if an e-commerce site shows 'Only 2 left!' when they have 200 in stock?" [Manipulation - it's deceptive scarcity]
- "If I use social proof by showing real customer reviews, is that ethical?" [Yes - it's truthful, transparent, and helpful information]
- "What makes reciprocity ethical vs. manipulative?" [Ethical: genuine value given freely with no pressure. Manipulative: creating obligation and demanding immediate return]
