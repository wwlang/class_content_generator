The Core Principle of Great Presentations

"Presentations rise or fall on the quality of the idea, the narrative, and the passion of the speaker. It's about substance, not style."

Chris Anderson, TED Curator (2013)

Speaker Notes

Opening:

  • Substance over style: No amount of fancy slides can compensate for weak ideas
  • Quality of idea: Must have something worth saying - research, insight, solution
  • Narrative: How you structure and tell the story (today's focus)

  • TED context: Anderson has coached thousands of speakers, identified these three as non-negotiable

  • Slides should SUPPORT your idea, not replace it

Nancy Duarte's Storytelling Framework

Beginning: Describe current reality (What is)

Reveal better future (What could be)

The "What is/What could be" Structure

Creates tension by contrasting present with possibility

Middle: Alternate between "What is" and "What could be"

Each cycle makes the future state more compelling

End: Describe "new bliss" - the transformed world

Call-to-action with concrete next steps

Duarte, N. (2012). Structure your presentation like a story. Harvard Business Review. https://hbr.org/2012/10/structure-your-presentation-li

Speaker Notes

Opening:

  • Draw on board: "What is ↔ What could be ↔ What is ↔ What could be → New Bliss"
  • Introduce Duarte: "Communication expert who analyzed great speeches - Martin Luther King Jr., Steve Jobs"

Key Teaching Points:

  • The contrast creates persuasion: By showing gap between reality and possibility, you create tension that needs resolution
  • Beginning: Establish rapport by describing their world accurately ("People should be nodding their heads")
  • Middle: Each alternation builds desire for the "what could be"
  • End: Don't just list tasks - paint picture of transformed world

Examples/Stories:

  • Real example: "Martin Luther King Jr.'s 'I Have a Dream' speech used this structure perfectly"
    • What is: Segregation, inequality, injustice
    • What could be: Children of different races playing together, judged by character not color
    • Alternated throughout, built to climactic vision of "new bliss"

Check for Understanding:

  • "Why does alternating between 'what is' and 'what could be' work better than just describing the future once?" [Answer: Repetition builds desire, contrast creates tension, audience gets increasingly attracted to "what could be"]

Anderson's Five Pillars Framework

CONTENT:

Chris Anderson, TED Curator, identifies five essential elements for great presentations (Anderson, 2013):

  1. Frame Your Story — Determine where to begin and conclude; position as a journey
  2. Plan Your Delivery — Choose: memorize, use bullets, or script (but never read)
  3. Develop Stage Presence — Focus on authenticity over perfection
  4. Plan Multimedia — Use visuals strategically; avoid reading slides
  5. Put It Together — Rehearse extensively; stay authentic

Key insight: Go deep, not broad. Avoid covering too much ground; use specific examples instead.

Anderson, C. (2013). How to give a killer presentation. Harvard Business Review, 91(6), 121-125. https://hbr.org/2013/06/how-to-give-a-killer-presentation

Speaker Notes

Opening:

  • Remind: "TED Talks = over 1 billion views, Anderson coached these speakers"
  • Context: "These five pillars apply whether you use Duarte's structure or any other framework"

Key Teaching Points:

  • Frame your story (#1): Most important - determines success or failure
  • Plan delivery (#2): Three options, but NEVER read from slides or script verbatim
  • Stage presence (#3): Authenticity beats polish (we'll practice next week)
  • Multimedia (#4): Slides should enhance, not replace your message
  • Rehearsal (#5): Non-negotiable for important presentations

Examples/Stories:

  • TED example: "Many successful TED speakers use no slides at all - the story is enough"
  • Vietnamese context: "In Vietnamese business culture, reading from slides is seen as unprepared - audience expects you to KNOW your content"

Common Misconceptions:

  • "I should memorize every word" → Only for very important talks; usually bullet points work better
  • "Perfect delivery matters most" → Substance + authenticity matter more than perfection

Check for Understanding:

  • "Why does Anderson say 'go deep, not broad'?" [Answer: Better to cover one topic thoroughly with examples than skim five topics superficially]
  • "What's wrong with reading from your slides?" [Audiences detect distance, lose engagement, create cognitive overload]

The Rule of Three in Presentations

CONTENT:

The Rule of Three: People understand and remember concepts better when presented in groups of three.

Why it works:

  • Cognitive processing: Our brains expect patterns of three - easier to process and recall
  • Narrative satisfaction: Beginning, middle, end feels complete
  • Persuasive rhythm: One point = weak, two = incomplete, three = convincing, four+ = overwhelming

Applications in presentations:

Structure: Divide into three main sections

Supporting points: Three reasons, three benefits, three examples per claim

Message hierarchy: Three key takeaways in summary

Example: This course organizes learning objectives into three categories (Understand & Apply, Analyze & Evaluate, Create & Present)

Speaker Notes

Opening:

  • Quick activity: "I'm going to say a list. Notice what feels complete: Red, blue. Red, blue, green. Red, blue, green, yellow." [Most will say three feels right]

Key Teaching Points:

  • Not arbitrary: Based on cognitive science - working memory comfortably holds ~3 chunks
  • Classic examples: "Life, liberty, pursuit of happiness" (US); "Liberty, equality, fraternity" (France); "Blood, sweat, and tears" (Churchill)
  • Business applications: "People, process, technology" common in consulting frameworks

Examples/Stories:

  • Vietnamese context: "FPT's 2024 focus areas: AI, semiconductors, green transformation - three pillars"
  • Student application: "Your Group Presentation should have ~3 main sections for 15 minutes"

Common Misconceptions:

  • "I have five important points - I must include all" → Choose the three most important; cut or combine the rest
  • "Three is just a superstition" → No, it's cognitive science (cite working memory research)

Check for Understanding:

  • "What are three ways you could apply the rule of three in your presentation?" [Answers: 3 sections, 3 examples per point, 3 takeaways, etc.]

Ten Simple Rules for Slide Design (Part 1)

Rules 1-5: Optimizing Slide Scope

  1. One idea per slide — Break complex concepts into progressive reveals
  2. One minute per slide — 20-minute talk = ~20 slides for good pacing
  3. Meaningful headlines — Transform "Results" into "CTNND1 is central to metastasis"
  4. Essential points only — Include only content you'll discuss; remove distractions
  5. Proper attribution — Always cite sources, names, credits consistently

Krammer, G., et al. (2021). Ten simple rules for effective presentation slides. PLOS Computational Biology, 17(12), e1009554.

Speaker Notes

Opening:

  • Context: "Published in peer-reviewed journal, based on cognitive science and teaching research"
  • Relevance: "These rules directly improve your Group Presentation slides"

Key Teaching Points:

  • Rule 1 (One idea): If explaining complex framework, show it piece by piece across 2-3 slides, not all at once
  • Rule 2 (One minute): Easy math - 15-minute presentation = ~15 slides
  • Rule 3 (Headlines): Your slide title should be the conclusion, not generic label
  • Rule 4 (Essential only): Delete anything you won't actually explain
  • Rule 5 (Attribution): Builds credibility, required for academic integrity

Examples/Stories:

  • Rule 1 violation: "I once saw a slide with a 12-step process diagram - impossible to absorb"
  • Rule 3 example: Instead of "Data Analysis," use "Sales increased 40% after campaign"
  • Vietnamese context: "FPT presentations use meaningful headlines - not 'Q4 Results' but 'Record Revenue Growth in AI Division'"

Common Misconceptions:

  • "I should put everything I want to say on slides so I don't forget" → Use speaker notes instead
  • "If I only put key points, slides will look empty" → Minimalism is professional and effective

Check for Understanding:

  • "How many slides should your 15-minute Group Presentation have?" [Answer: ~15, using one-minute rule]
  • "What's better: slide titled 'Benefits' or 'Three Ways This Saves Time'?" [Answer: Second - meaningful headline]

Transition:

  • "Rules 1-5 were about WHAT to put on slides. Rules 6-8 are about HOW to design them..."

Ten Simple Rules for Slide Design (Part 2)

Design specifics (Rule 7)

  • High-contrast colors, simple backgrounds
  • Sans serif fonts, large sizes (avoid italics, ALL CAPS)
  • Color-blind accessible palettes
  • ≤6 elements per slide

Krammer, G., et al. (2021). Ten simple rules for effective presentation slides. PLOS Computational Biology, 17(12), e1009554.

Speaker Notes

Opening:

  • Remind about cognitive overload: "Reading and listening compete - same brain channel"
  • Show example: Display slide with just image + 3-word caption while talking - much easier to process

Key Teaching Points:

  • Rule 6 (Graphics): One powerful image > 100 words; but don't overcrowd
  • Rule 7 (Avoid overload): Key phrase "short text fragments, redundant with verbal" - repeat what you say, don't add new info
  • Rule 8 (Distraction-resistant): Main message should be clear even without narration
  • Font choice: Sans serif (Arial, Helvetica, Calibri) more readable than serif (Times New Roman) on screens

Examples/Stories:

  • Vietnamese context: "Many Vietnamese presentations use decorative backgrounds - research says simple is better"

  • "Fancy fonts look more professional" → Simple sans serif fonts are most professional for presentations

Ten Simple Rules for Slide Design (Part 3)

Rules 9-10: Presentation Preparation

  1. Iterative practice — Rehearse with live audiences; reveals timing issues, weak transitions
  2. Technical disaster mitigation — Save as PDF backup; avoid animations; screenshot videos

Why practice matters:

  • Reveals which slides take longer than expected
  • Identifies confusing transitions
  • Exposes Rule 2, 4, 5 violations (too many/too few slides, extra content, missing credits)

Why avoid animations:

  • "No one likes them" (research surveys)
  • Create accessibility issues for visually impaired
  • Introduce software compatibility risks
  • Add no real value

Krammer, G., et al. (2021). Ten simple rules for effective presentation slides. PLOS Computational Biology, 17(12), e1009554.

Speaker Notes

Opening:

  • Emphasize: "Rules 1-8 won't matter if you don't practice (#9) or your tech fails (#10)"
  • Connect to their assessment: "Your Group Presentation requires rehearsal time - plan for it"

Key Teaching Points:

  • Rule 9 (Practice): Practice alone first, then with friend/classmate - fresh eyes catch issues
  • Rule 10 (Tech backup): Murphy's Law in action - if it can fail, it will (especially fonts, videos)
  • Animation advice: Controversial but research-backed - avoid fancy transitions
  • PDF backup: Ensures fonts, layout preserved across computers

Examples/Stories:

  • Practice story: "Group last semester practiced once the night before - discovered they were 8 minutes over time limit, had to cut frantically"
  • Tech failure story: "Student's embedded video wouldn't play on classroom computer - 5 minutes wasted troubleshooting"

Opening Hooks: Five Proven Techniques

CONTENT:

Your opening 30-60 seconds determines audience engagement for the entire presentation.

Five effective hook techniques:

  1. Provocative Question — "What would you do if your company's data was breached right now?"
  2. Surprising Statistic — "90% of online courses are never completed - here's why"
  3. Relevant Story — Brief anecdote (30-45 seconds) that illustrates your topic
  4. Bold Statement — "Everything you learned about email communication is wrong"
  5. Visual Hook — Powerful image that creates curiosity without explanation (yet)

What NOT to do:

❌ "Today I'm going to talk about..." (boring)

❌ Long apologies or self-deprecation

❌ Excessive background/introduction before getting to point

Speaker Notes

Opening:

  • Remind: "Your opening hook is the first element of Duarte's structure - it establishes 'what is' and creates tension"
  • Show examples from your own presentation: "Notice I started today with a question about boring presentations - that was a hook"

Key Teaching Points:

  • 30-second rule: Research shows attention decisions made quickly
  • Hook = tension: You're creating a gap (curiosity, concern, interest) that your presentation will resolve
  • Choose ONE technique: Don't combine multiple hooks - pick your strongest
  • Avoid apologies: "I'm not prepared" or "This might be boring" kills engagement immediately

Examples/Stories:

  • Provocative question: Chris Anderson's TED talk on presentations opens with "What makes presentations effective?"
  • Surprising stat: "Did you know 67% of employees say poor communication is their biggest workplace frustration?"
  • Story example: "When Steve Jobs introduced iPhone, he started: 'Every once in a while, a revolutionary product comes along that changes everything'"
  • Vietnamese context: "In Vietnamese business culture, starting with too much self-deprecation can undermine credibility - balance humility with confidence"

Common Misconceptions:

  • "I should introduce myself and my credentials first" → Brief intro OK, but hook comes first
  • "Story hooks should be long and detailed" → 30-45 seconds maximum, just enough to create interest

Check for Understanding:

  • "Which hook technique would work best for a presentation on crisis communication?" [Answers could vary: provocative question "What would you do if...?", story about real crisis, surprising stat about crisis failures]
  • "Why should you avoid starting with 'Today I'm going to talk about...'?" [Answer: Boring, no tension, audience knows you're talking to them already]

Transition:

  • "You've hooked them at the start. Now you need to land the ending with a strong call-to-action..."

Closing Calls-to-Action (CTAs)

CONTENT:

Your closing should inspire action, not just summarize. Strong CTAs are specific, achievable, and aligned with your presentation's purpose.

Three-part closing structure:

  1. Synthesize (30 seconds) — Briefly reinforce 2-3 key takeaways (rule of three)
  2. Inspire (30 seconds) — Paint "new bliss" vision (Duarte) of transformed future
  3. Direct (30 seconds) — Specific call-to-action with concrete next steps

Examples of specific CTAs:

  • Persuasive: "Schedule a meeting with your team this week to discuss implementation"
  • Informative: "Review the handout and try technique #2 in your next email"
  • Inspirational: "Commit to one small change today - message me what you choose"

Weak CTAs to avoid:

❌ "So that's everything - any questions?"

❌ "Thank you for listening" [ends abruptly]

❌ "Let me know if you want to learn more"

Speaker Notes

Opening:

  • Remind: "Audiences remember beginnings and endings most - make them count"
  • Connect to Duarte: "The CTA is where you deliver the 'new bliss' and tell them how to get there"

Key Teaching Points:

  • Three-part structure: Synthesize → Inspire → Direct (another rule of three)
  • Specific, not vague: "Email me your questions" is better than "Let me know if interested"
  • Achievable action: Don't ask for huge commitment; one small step is better
  • Align with purpose: Persuasive presentations need stronger, more direct CTAs

Examples/Stories:

  • Strong CTA example: "Your Group Presentation CTA might be: 'Before next class, identify one communication habit you'll change based on what we shared today'"
  • Weak CTA story: "Student presentation ended with 'Thanks for listening' - no clear takeaway, audience forgot content within 5 minutes"
  • Vietnamese context: "In Vietnamese culture, being too direct can seem pushy - frame CTAs as invitations: 'I invite you to try...' vs. 'You must...'"

Common Misconceptions:

  • "Questions should come after my last point" → No, deliver CTA first, THEN invite questions
  • "Thank you for listening is a good closing" → It's polite but weak - use it AFTER your CTA
  • "My CTA should be big and ambitious" → Small, specific actions have higher compliance

Check for Understanding:

Creating Your Presentation Storyboard

CONTENT:

Storyboard = Blueprint for your presentation before designing slides

Five-step storyboard process:

  1. Define purpose — Persuade, inform, or inspire? (Choose primary)
  2. Choose structure — Duarte "what is/what could be" OR Anderson's 5 pillars OR rule of three sections
  3. Craft opening hook — Select technique (question, stat, story, statement, visual)
  4. Outline body — List key points (≤6), assign ~1 minute per slide
  5. Design closing CTA — Synthesize, inspire, direct (three parts)

Tutorial preview: You'll practice this process by creating a storyboard for a 5-minute presentation on a communication topic.

Key Takeaways

CONTENT:

Three essential principles for effective presentations:

  1. Substance over style — Quality of idea + narrative + passion (Anderson, 2013)
    • Choose persuade, inform, or inspire as primary purpose
    • Structure with Duarte's "what is/what could be" or rule of three
  2. Design for cognition — Avoid cognitive overload (Krammer et al., 2021)
    • ≤6 elements per slide
    • Graphics over text
    • One idea per slide, one minute per slide
  3. Hook and land — Strong openings and closings are critical
    • Hook techniques: question, stat, story, statement, visual
    • CTA structure: synthesize → inspire → direct
