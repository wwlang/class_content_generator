# SEGMENT 1

## Self-Regulated Learning Theory

*The science of taking control of your learning*

<speaker-notes>
We start with self-regulated learning (SRL) theory because it's the most researched framework for understanding how successful learners think and act differently from struggling learners.

Self-regulated learning isn't about intelligence or natural ability‚Äîit's about using specific strategies to plan, monitor, and adjust your learning. This is great news: it means anyone can become a better learner by adopting these strategies.

Barry Zimmerman's work at the City University of New York has been foundational. His 2002 article we're reading this week has over 3,300 citations‚Äîit's the definitive introduction to SRL.

Key insight: struggling students often believe successful students are just "smarter." Research shows successful students use different strategies‚Äîstrategies you can learn.
</speaker-notes>

</slide>

<slide number="7" layout="framework" title="What Is Self-Regulated Learning?">

## Self-Regulated Learning Definition

**"The degree to which students are metacognitively, motivationally, and behaviorally active participants in their own learning process."**
*(Zimmerman, 2002)*

**Three key elements:**

1. **Metacognitive:** Awareness and control of thinking processes
2. **Motivational:** Self-efficacy beliefs and goal orientation
3. **Behavioral:** Selecting and using strategies; seeking help when needed

**SRL is cyclical, not linear‚Äîcontinuous adjustment based on feedback.**

<speaker-notes>
Zimmerman's definition emphasizes three dimensions working together.

Metacognitive means you're aware of HOW you learn, not just WHAT you learn. You monitor comprehension, recognize when strategies aren't working, and adjust approaches.

Motivational addresses beliefs about your capability. Students with high self-efficacy ("I can improve through effort") persist longer when challenged than students with fixed mindset ("I'm just not good at this").

Behavioral refers to concrete actions: choosing study strategies, managing time, seeking help from mentors or peers when stuck.

The cyclical nature is crucial. Self-regulated learners don't just plan once‚Äîthey continuously monitor progress and adjust strategies based on what's working. Linear thinkers plan once and execute rigidly even when it's not effective.

Vietnamese educational culture often emphasizes behavioral compliance but may underemphasize metacognitive awareness. SRL frameworks develop both.
</speaker-notes>

FORETHOUGHT PHASE occurs BEFORE you start studying. PERFORMANCE PHASE happens DURING studying. This includes: (1) Self-control strategies‚Äîfocusing attention, using imagery, self-instruction ("First I'll identify the three phases..."), and (2) Self-observation‚Äîself-recording (tracking study time), self-experimentation (trying different techniques).

The cycle then feeds back: Your reflection informs the next forethought phase. If your strategy worked, you refine it. If it didn't, you adapt.

This contrasts with common student approaches: study randomly without planning, don't monitor whether strategies work, never reflect on effectiveness.

## Forethought Phase: Before You Start

**Task Analysis:**
- **Goal setting:** Specific, proximal goals (not vague, distant ones)
- **Strategic planning:** Selecting appropriate learning methods

**Self-Motivation Beliefs:**
- **Self-efficacy:** Confidence in your capability to learn this material
- **Outcome expectations:** Belief that effort will lead to desired results
- **Task interest/value:** Why this matters for your goals
- **Goal orientation:** Learning goals vs. performance goals

**Example:** "I'll master Zimmerman's three phases [goal] by creating a concept map and teaching it to a peer [strategy] because it's essential for my PDP [value]."

<speaker-notes>
The forethought phase is where self-regulated learners distinguish themselves most clearly from passive learners.

Goal setting research shows specific, proximal goals work better than vague, distant ones. Bad goal: "Understand self-regulated learning." Good goal: "By Friday, I can explain Zimmerman's three phases with examples from my own learning."

Strategic planning means matching strategies to goals. If your goal is deep understanding, rereading is insufficient‚Äîyou need elaboration, self-testing, concept mapping.

Self-motivation beliefs are powerful predictors of persistence. Students with strong self-efficacy persist through difficulty. Students with fixed mindset ("I'm bad at psychology concepts") quit when challenged.

Outcome expectations matter too. If you believe effort won't help ("This is just memorization for the exam"), you won't invest deeply. When you see learning as personally relevant, motivation increases.

Goal orientation distinguishes learning goals (mastery-focused) from performance goals (grade-focused). Research shows learning goals lead to deeper engagement and better long-term retention.

Vietnamese students often have strong performance goal orientation due to competitive educational culture. SRL encourages balancing this with learning goals‚Äîunderstanding matters beyond grades.
</speaker-notes>

</slide>

<slide number="10" layout="content" title="Performance Phase Details">

## Performance Phase: While You're Learning

**Self-Control Strategies:**
- **Imagery:** Creating mental pictures to understand concepts
- **Self-instruction:** Talking yourself through processes
- **Attention focusing:** Eliminating distractions; deep work blocks
- **Task strategies:** Using proven techniques (we'll cover these in Segment 3)

**Self-Observation:**
- **Self-recording:** Tracking time, strategies used, progress
- **Self-experimentation:** Testing which approaches work best for you

**Example:** During study session, eliminate phone [attention focusing], use Pomodoro technique [task strategy], track which concepts require more review [self-recording].

<speaker-notes>
The performance phase is where strategies translate to action. Many students skip strategic thinking and jump straight to "studying"‚Äîbut without self-control and self-observation, they're learning inefficiently.

Self-control strategies help you manage the learning process. Imagery works particularly well for complex frameworks‚Äîvisualizing Zimmerman's cycle helps you remember it. Self-instruction means talking through steps: "First I identify the phase, then I list its components..."

Attention focusing is critical in our distracted digital age. Newport's concept of "deep work"‚Äîfocused, undistracted blocks‚Äîapplies here. Most students study with phones nearby, social media open, constantly interrupted. Self-regulated learners create focus-conducive environments.

Task strategies refers to specific learning techniques. We'll explore evidence-based options in Segment 3‚Äîretrieval practice, spaced practice, elaboration. Self-regulated learners use proven techniques, not habits adopted without evaluation.

Self-observation means monitoring while learning. Self-recording might be: "I spent 45 minutes on Zimmerman article, made notes, tested myself‚Äî80% recall." Self-experimentation: "Concept mapping worked better than linear notes for understanding relationships."

The key distinction: passive students study without awareness of effectiveness. Self-regulated learners continuously monitor and adjust.
</speaker-notes>

</slide>

## Self-Reflection Phase: After Learning

**Self-Judgment:**
- **Self-evaluation:** Did I achieve my goal? To what extent?
- **Causal attribution:** Why did I succeed or struggle?
  - Adaptive: "I succeeded because I used active strategies"
  - Maladaptive: "I succeeded because it was easy" or "I failed because I'm not smart"

**Self-Reaction:**
- **Self-satisfaction/affect:** Emotional response to performance
- **Adaptive responses:** Adjusting strategies for next time
- **Defensive responses:** Avoiding challenges to protect self-image

**Example:** "I mastered two phases but struggled with self-reflection details [evaluation]. I need more elaboration and examples [attribution]. Next time I'll create more self-test questions [adaptive response]."

<speaker-notes>
The self-reflection phase closes the loop by evaluating effectiveness and informing the next cycle's forethought phase.

Self-evaluation asks: Did you meet your goal? Be specific. Not "I studied," but "I can now explain all three phases and give examples‚Äî80% of my goal achieved."

Causal attribution is psychologically crucial. Students who attribute success to effective strategies build self-efficacy: "My method worked‚ÄîI'll refine it." Students who attribute success to external factors ("easy material," "lucky questions") don't learn what to repeat.

Similarly, adaptive attribution for struggles is: "I used ineffective strategies‚ÄîI'll try different ones." Maladaptive attribution is: "I'm just not good at this type of content"‚Äîthis kills motivation and prevents improvement.

Self-reaction refers to emotional and behavioral responses. Self-satisfaction motivates continued effort. But the key is adaptive versus defensive responses. Adaptive: "That didn't work‚ÄîI'll try a different approach." Defensive: "I'll avoid difficult topics to feel better about myself."

Vietnamese students sometimes exhibit defensive responses to protect face‚Äîavoiding situations where they might struggle. SRL frameworks normalize struggle as learning opportunity and emphasize strategy adjustment over ability judgments.

The cycle then repeats: Your self-reflection informs next time's goal-setting and strategic planning.
</speaker-notes>

# SEGMENT 2

## Self-Directed Learning in Practice

*From theory to actionable steps*

<speaker-notes>
Segment 2 translates self-regulated learning theory into practical process you can implement immediately. We're moving from "what" and "why" to "how."

The University of Waterloo's Centre for Teaching Excellence developed a four-step self-directed learning process that's become widely used in higher education. It's particularly valuable because it provides concrete structure while building autonomy.

Self-directed learning (SDL) and self-regulated learning (SRL) are related but distinct. SRL focuses on the cognitive and motivational processes‚Äîthe internal mechanisms. SDL focuses on the external process‚Äîhow you structure learning experiences when you're in control rather than instructor-directed.

Your Mentorship Program is self-directed learning in action‚Äîyou control the agenda, you identify learning needs, you seek resources (your mentor). The four-step process structures this autonomy.
</speaker-notes>

Vietnamese educational culture often emphasizes teacher-directed learning. SDL develops complementary capabilities‚Äîyou still respect expertise and seek guidance, but you also take initiative and ownership.
</speaker-notes>

## Step 1: Assess Readiness to Learn

**Three dimensions to assess:**

**1. Attitudes:** Are you curious? Motivated? Open to learning?
**2. Abilities:** Do you have foundational skills? Time management? Learning strategies?
**3. Characteristics:** Are you self-disciplined? Persistent? Comfortable with ambiguity?

**Honest self-assessment identifies gaps so you can seek support.**

**Key insight:** SDL readiness can be developed‚Äîlow readiness now doesn't mean you can't do SDL with appropriate scaffolding.

<speaker-notes>
Step 1 is honest self-assessment across three dimensions.

ATTITUDES: Self-directed learners are curious, motivated, and open-minded. They see learning as personally relevant, not just compliance. If your attitude is "I'll learn what I'm told to learn," you're not yet ready for full self-direction‚Äîbut you can develop SDL attitudes.

ABILITIES: Do you have core skills? Time management (can you structure your own schedule?), learning strategies (do you know how to learn new content?), information literacy (can you evaluate source quality?). If gaps exist, you need to build these abilities or seek structured support.

CHARACTERISTICS: Self-discipline means working without external pressure. Persistence means continuing when challenged. Tolerance for ambiguity means being comfortable when the path isn't fully clear. These characteristics can be developed through practice.

The assessment isn't pass/fail. Low readiness means: "I need more scaffolding‚Äîmore structure, more checkpoints, more guidance." Medium readiness means: "I can self-direct with some support." High readiness means: "I can operate independently with minimal guidance."

For your Mentorship Program, most first-year students have low-to-medium SDL readiness. That's why we provide structure: preparation forms, reflection prompts, scheduled meetings. As you practice, readiness increases.

Key mindset shift: viewing SDL readiness as developable, not fixed. You're not "a self-directed person" or "not a self-directed person"‚Äîyou develop these capabilities through practice.
</speaker-notes>

## Step 2: Set Learning Goals (Learning Contracts)

**Learning Contract Components:**

| Element | Question | Example |
|---------|----------|---------|
| **Learning goals** | What will I learn? | "I will understand digital marketing career pathways and required competencies" |
| **Strategies/Resources** | How will I learn it? | "Mentorship meetings, LinkedIn research, informational interviews" |
| **Evidence of accomplishment** | How will I know I learned it? | "I can describe 3 career paths with pros/cons and required skills" |
| **Timeline** | By when? | "By Week 8 mentorship meeting" |

**Learning contracts create accountability and clarity.**

<speaker-notes>
Step 2 translates vague goals into specific learning contracts. This addresses a common student struggle: "I want to improve my career readiness" is too vague to act on or evaluate. Learning contracts force specificity.

LEARNING GOALS answer "What will I learn?" Be specific. Not "learn about marketing," but "understand three specific digital marketing career pathways in Vietnam and the competencies required for each."

STRATEGIES AND RESOURCES answer "How will I learn it?" List specific actions. For career pathways: (1) discuss with mentor who has marketing experience, (2) research companies on LinkedIn, (3) conduct informational interview with marketing professional. Having multiple strategies reduces dependence on any single source.

EVIDENCE OF ACCOMPLISHMENT is crucial‚Äîit makes goals measurable. "I can describe three career paths, listing pros/cons of each and competencies required, with specific company examples." This clarity lets you definitively evaluate whether you achieved the goal.

TIMELINE creates urgency and accountability. "By Week 8 mentorship meeting" gives you a specific deadline and sets up natural evaluation point.

For your PDP assessment, learning contracts should be included for each SMART goal. You're essentially creating agreements with yourself (and your mentor) about what you'll accomplish.

Learning contracts are common in professional development, executive education, and MBA programs. You're learning a tool you'll use throughout your career‚Äînot just for this course.
</speaker-notes>

## Step 3: Engage in the Learning Process

**Active engagement strategies:**

**Identify diverse resources:**
- Human resources: mentors, peers, professionals, instructors
- Information resources: articles, books, online courses, podcasts
- Experiential resources: field trips, guest speakers, practice opportunities

**Select appropriate strategies:**
- Match strategies to learning goals (we'll cover evidence-based strategies in Segment 3)
- Use active approaches (testing, elaborating, discussing) over passive ones (rereading, highlighting)

**Monitor progress continuously:**
- Are you moving toward your goal? How do you know?
- What's working? What's not? What adjustments are needed?

<speaker-notes>
Step 3 is the "doing" phase‚Äîimplementing your learning contract.

IDENTIFY DIVERSE RESOURCES: Self-directed learners don't rely on single sources. For career exploration, you might use: (1) your mentor's expertise, (2) LinkedIn research on professionals' career trajectories, (3) articles on industry trends, (4) conversations with professionals at field trips, (5) peer discussions about career interests. Multiple perspectives create richer understanding.

Vietnamese students sometimes rely heavily on teacher as sole authority. SDL develops information-seeking skills‚Äîknowing what resources exist and how to access them. Your mentor is one expert source, but you should also seek diverse perspectives.

SELECT APPROPRIATE STRATEGIES: We'll cover evidence-based strategies in detail in Segment 3. The key principle: active strategies beat passive ones. To learn about career pathways, passive approach is reading job descriptions. Active approach is: (1) creating comparison matrix of pathways, (2) conducting informational interviews, (3) explaining pathways to a peer, (4) testing yourself on key competencies for each path.

MONITOR PROGRESS CONTINUOUSLY: This is Zimmerman's self-observation during the performance phase. Ask regularly: "Am I making progress? How do I know? What's the evidence?" ## Metacognition: Thinking About Thinking

**"Thinking about one's own thinking"** *(Flavell, 1979)*

**Three components (Stanton et al., 2021):**

1. **Metacognitive knowledge:** What you know about how you learn
   - Self-knowledge: "I learn concepts better through examples than definitions"
   - Task knowledge: "Understanding theories requires elaboration and application"
   - Strategy knowledge: "Self-testing reveals gaps better than rereading"

2. **Metacognitive monitoring:** Assessing your current understanding
   - "Do I really understand this, or does it just feel familiar?"
   - Calibration: Accuracy of self-assessment

3. **Metacognitive control:** Adjusting strategies based on monitoring
   - "This isn't working‚ÄîI'll try a different approach"

<speaker-notes>
Metacognition is "thinking about thinking"‚Äîawareness and control of your cognitive processes. John Flavell coined the term in 1979. It's now recognized as one of the most powerful predictors of academic success.

The three components work together:

METACOGNITIVE KNOWLEDGE is what you know about learning in general and your learning specifically. Self-knowledge: "I concentrate better in morning hours, so I'll study difficult content then." Task knowledge: "Understanding theoretical frameworks requires more than memorization‚ÄîI need to see applications." Strategy knowledge: "I remember information better when I organize it visually in concept maps."

This knowledge develops through experience and reflection. First-year students typically have limited metacognitive knowledge‚Äîthey haven't yet learned what works for them. By final year, effective students have extensive self-knowledge.

METACOGNITIVE MONITORING is real-time assessment of understanding. Poor monitors have "illusions of competence"‚Äîthey reread notes, feel familiar with content, and assume they've mastered it. Then they perform poorly on exams. Strong monitors use testing to calibrate understanding: "I thought I understood self-regulated learning, but when I tried to explain it without notes, I couldn't. I need more practice."

CALIBRATION refers to accuracy of self-assessment. Overconfident students think they know more than they do. Underconfident students know more than they believe. Accurate calibration helps you allocate study time effectively‚Äîspend more time on what you haven't mastered, less on what you have.

METACOGNITIVE CONTROL is strategy adjustment based on monitoring. If self-testing reveals gaps, you adjust: "I'll create more practice questions focusing on self-reflection phase." If approach isn't working, you try alternatives: "Concept mapping isn't helping with this content‚ÄîI'll try elaborative interrogation instead."

These three components create a feedback loop: knowledge informs monitoring, monitoring triggers control, control builds knowledge.
</speaker-notes>

## Critical Evaluation: The Learning Styles Myth

**The claim:** Matching instruction to students' learning styles (visual, auditory, kinesthetic) improves learning.

**The evidence:** After decades of research, **no empirical support exists** for this "meshing hypothesis."

**Why it persists despite being debunked:**
- Feels intuitively right
- Confirms people's preferences
- Widely promoted in popular education literature

**What's true instead:**
- You have learning **preferences** (comfort), not learning **styles** (effectiveness)
- Multiple modalities benefit everyone, regardless of preference
- Evidence-based strategies work for all learners

**Bottom line:** Stop choosing strategies based on "your learning style." Use evidence-based strategies instead.

*(Pashler et al., 2008; Newton & Salvi, 2020; Stanton et al., 2021)*

<speaker-notes>
We need to address learning styles directly because it's one of the most persistent educational myths, widely believed despite lack of evidence.

THE CLAIM: VARK model says people are Visual, Auditory, Reading/Writing, or Kinesthetic learners. Kolb's model identifies four learning styles. The meshing hypothesis claims: if instruction matches your style, you learn better. If visual learner receives visual instruction, learning improves.

THE EVIDENCE: Psychologists Pashler, McDaniel, Roediger, and Bjork reviewed all learning styles research in 2008‚Äîpublished in Psychological Science in the Public Interest. Their conclusion: "We found virtually no evidence" supporting the meshing hypothesis. Subsequent research has confirmed: matching instruction to supposed learning style doesn't improve learning.

Studies testing this directly: Give students a learning style assessment, then teach them new content either matched to their style or not matched. Result: NO DIFFERENCE in learning outcomes between matched and mismatched conditions.

WHY IT PERSISTS: Newton and Salvi (2020) found 89-90% of teachers worldwide still believe learning styles improve learning. Why? (1) Feels intuitively right‚Äîpeople do have preferences. (2) Confirms our self-perceptions. (3) Massively promoted in popular education books and teacher training.

WHAT'S TRUE INSTEAD: You have preferences‚Äîways you feel comfortable learning. But preference doesn't equal effectiveness. I might prefer learning by listening to podcasts, but testing myself works better for long-term retention even though it's less comfortable.

Multiple modalities benefit EVERYONE. Visual diagrams help all students understand relationships, not just "visual learners." Discussing concepts helps all students, not just "verbal learners."

Evidence-based strategies‚Äîwhich we'll cover next‚Äîwork regardless of learning style preferences. This is great news: you don't need to figure out your style, you just need to use proven strategies.
</speaker-notes>

## What Actually Works: Evidence-Based Strategies

**HIGH EFFECTIVENESS (Dunlosky et al., 2013):**

1. **Retrieval Practice (Self-Testing)**
   - Quiz yourself without notes; use flashcards; teach someone else
   - Why it works: Retrieving strengthens memory more than restudying

2. **Spaced Practice (Distributed Practice)**
   - Study across multiple sessions over time instead of cramming
   - Why it works: Spacing forces memory consolidation and reconsolidation

**MODERATE EFFECTIVENESS:**

3. **Elaboration**
   - Ask "why" and "how" questions; connect to what you know; use examples
   - Why it works: Creates meaningful connections and deeper processing

4. **Interleaving**
   - Mix different topics/problems during study instead of blocking by topic
   - Why it works: Forces discrimination between concepts and builds flexible understanding

<speaker-notes>
Now we get to the practical payoff: what learning strategies actually work according to cognitive science research.

Dunlosky and colleagues evaluated 10 popular learning techniques in 2013, published in Psychological Science in the Public Interest. They rated effectiveness based on: (1) Does research show it improves learning? (2) Does it work across different types of content? (3) Does it work for different types of students? (4) Is it practical to implement?

HIGH EFFECTIVENESS‚Äîuse these consistently:

RETRIEVAL PRACTICE means actively recalling information from memory without looking at notes. Methods: (1) Self-quizzing‚Äîclose book, write everything you remember. (2) Flashcards‚Äîtest yourself, not just review. (3) Practice problems without solution guides. (4) Teach concept to someone else‚Äîexplaining reveals gaps.

Why effective: The "testing effect"‚Äîretrieving information strengthens memory more than restudying. Every time you successfully retrieve "Zimmerman's three phases are forethought, performance, self-reflection," you strengthen those neural pathways. Rereading your notes doesn't provide retrieval practice‚Äîyou're not pulling from memory, you're recognizing familiar text.

SPACED PRACTICE means studying across multiple sessions separated by time instead of one long session. Study Zimmerman article Monday, review Wednesday, test yourself Friday, final review next Monday‚Äîinstead of 4-hour marathon Sunday night.

Why effective: Spacing forces memory consolidation. When you space practice, you partially forget between sessions, then have to actively reconstruct understanding‚Äîthat reconsolidation strengthens learning. Cramming produces short-term familiarity but weak long-term retention.

MODERATE EFFECTIVENESS‚Äîalso valuable:

ELABORATION means asking questions that force deeper processing: "Why does self-reflection phase connect back to forethought?" "How does this apply to my mentorship meetings?" "What's an example from my experience?" Creating your own examples and connecting to prior knowledge deepens understanding.

INTERLEAVING means mixing different topics during study. Instead of studying all Zimmerman, then all Waterloo, then all metacognition‚Äîstudy 20 minutes Zimmerman, 20 minutes Waterloo, 20 minutes metacognition, repeat. Mixing forces you to actively identify which framework applies when.

Notice what's absent: REREADING, HIGHLIGHTING, SUMMARIZING are rated LOW effectiveness despite being most popular. These create illusions of competence‚Äîfamiliarity without deep understanding.
</speaker-notes>
