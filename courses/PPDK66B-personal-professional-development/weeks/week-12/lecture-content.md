<slide number="4" layout="section-break" title="Part 1: Personal Mastery">
# Part 1: Personal Mastery
## Peter Senge's Discipline of Continuous Growth

<speaker-notes>
Peter Senge's "The Fifth Discipline" (1990, revised 2006) is a seminal work on learning organizations. Chapter 9, "Personal Mastery," applies organizational learning principles to individual development. Harvard Business Review called it "one of the seminal management books of the previous 75 years."

Senge defines personal mastery as "the discipline of personal growth and learning." It's NOT about dominating or perfection—it's about continuously clarifying and deepening personal vision. Mastery is a PROCESS, not a destination.

Why this matters: Your PDP has 1-year, 3-year, 5-year goals—but what about Year 6? Year 20? Personal mastery provides the philosophy for LIFELONG development. The core of Senge's model is creative tension.
</speaker-notes>
</slide>

<slide number="5" layout="framework" title="Senge's Creative Tension Model">
# Creative Tension = Growth Engine

**PERSONAL VISION** (Where you want to be)
↕ **GAP = CREATIVE TENSION**
**CURRENT REALITY** (Where you are now)

*"The gap between vision and current reality generates energy for change."*
— Peter Senge (2006)

<speaker-notes>
This is the CORE concept of personal mastery. The gap between where you are and where you want to be creates energy—like a stretched rubber band.

Three components: (1) Personal Vision—your aspirations, what truly matters, your desired future. This is deeper than goals. (2) Current Reality—honest assessment of where you are NOW, using Week 3 reflection skills and Week 5 self-assessments. (3) Creative Tension—the natural tendency to resolve the gap by moving toward vision.

Connection to your PDP: Your SWOT analysis (Part 2) assesses current reality honestly. Your vision statement (Part 3) clarifies personal vision. Your SMART goals are specific steps to resolve the tension.

Example: Current Reality is "I'm an undergraduate with limited public speaking experience, anxious presenting to groups." Personal Vision is "I want to inspire and influence through compelling communication." Creative Tension motivates joining Toastmasters, seeking presentation opportunities, and practicing deliberately.

Critical nuance: Tension is CREATIVE (energizing) not destructive (stressful) when you're committed to both truth and vision. Most people avoid tension by either lowering their vision (settling for less) or lying about current reality (self-deception). Personal mastery requires holding BOTH honestly.

But there's a trap—what Senge calls structural conflict.
</speaker-notes>
</slide>

<slide number="6" layout="content" title="Structural Conflict">
# The Hidden Obstacle

**Creative Tension pushes you toward your vision...**
**BUT structural conflict pulls you back**

**Two competing beliefs:**
1. **"I want to achieve my vision"** (creative tension)
2. **"I'm not worthy / capable of achieving it"** (structural conflict)

*Result: Self-sabotage, settling for mediocrity, or giving up*

**Overcoming Structural Conflict:**
- Tell the truth about limiting beliefs
- Choose commitment to vision over powerlessness
- Use Week 9 Growth Mindset: abilities can be developed

<speaker-notes>
This explains why New Year's resolutions fail and why talented people underachieve. It's not lack of goals—it's unconscious belief in unworthiness or powerlessness.

Structural Conflict means competing commitments that undermine creative tension. Two forms: (1) Powerlessness—"I can't really achieve this" (external locus of control). (2) Unworthiness—"I don't deserve this success" (imposter syndrome).

Connection to the course: Week 9 Growth Mindset vs. Fixed Mindset addresses powerlessness beliefs. Week 5 Self-Assessment plus Mentorship builds evidence of worthiness. Week 7 Emotional Intelligence teaches managing limiting beliefs.

Real example: A student creates an amazing PDP with the goal "VP of Marketing by age 30." But unconsciously believes "People like me don't become VPs" due to family background, gender, or nationality. Result: Sabotages opportunities, doesn't apply for promotions, settles for less. Solution: Name the belief, challenge it with evidence, reframe the narrative.

In tutorial today, we'll identify structural conflicts in your PDPs—beliefs that might sabotage you.

Personal mastery also requires integrating reason with intuition—what Senge calls subconscious learning.
</speaker-notes>

<slide number="9" layout="section-break" title="Part 2: Self-Determination Theory">
# Part 2: Self-Determination Theory
## The Science Behind Drive

<speaker-notes>
You've probably read or watched Dan Pink's "Drive" (2009)—it's hugely popular. Pink based his framework on Self-Determination Theory (SDT). But he oversimplified and made errors—today we get the science right.

SDT represents 40+ years of empirical research by Deci, Ryan, and collaborators. Van den Broeck et al. (2021) conducted a meta-analysis of 124 studies validating SDT. Understanding TRUE motivation science helps you design careers that energize rather than drain you.

Pink said motivation needs Autonomy, Mastery, and Purpose. He was MOSTLY right, but missed key nuances.
</speaker-notes>
</slide>

<slide number="10" layout="content" title="Pink vs. SDT">
# Drive (Pink, 2009) vs. Self-Determination Theory (Research)

**Pink's Framework:**
- Autonomy
- Mastery
- Purpose

**Self-Determination Theory (Original):**
- Autonomy
- Competence (not mastery)
- Relatedness (NOT purpose)

**Why it matters:** Pink substituted "purpose" for "relatedness" and oversimplified extrinsic motivation

<speaker-notes>
This isn't about bashing Pink—his book made motivation science accessible. But as professionals, you need the actual research, not pop psychology.

Key differences: (1) Competence vs. Mastery. Pink used "Mastery" meaning the urge to get better at meaningful work. SDT uses "Competence" meaning feeling effective and capable. Subtle difference, but mastery implies perfection while competence is about feeling capable.

(2) Purpose vs. Relatedness. Pink emphasized "Purpose"—desire to serve something larger than self. SDT identifies "Relatedness"—connection to others, belonging, meaningful relationships. Pink changed this because "purpose" sounds better in business books. But research shows RELATIONSHIPS are the third psychological need, not abstract purpose.

(3) Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic. Pink suggests extrinsic motivation (rewards, money) is BAD and intrinsic is GOOD. SDT reveals it's way more complex—SIX types of motivation on a continuum.

Why this matters for your career: If you only chase "purpose" but neglect relationships, you risk burnout. If you demonize all extrinsic motivation, you miss important career realities.

Let's look at what SDT actually says about the six types of motivation.
</speaker-notes>
</slide>

<slide number="11" layout="framework" title="Self-Determination Continuum">
# Six Types of Motivation (Not Just Two)

**Low Autonomy ←――――――――――――――――――――――――→ High Autonomy**

1. **Amotivation** — No motivation ("I don't care")
2. **External Regulation** — Rewards/punishments ("I do it for the paycheck")
3. **Introjected Regulation** — Guilt/ego ("I should to avoid shame")
4. **Identified Regulation** — Personal meaning ("This aligns with my values")
5. **Integrated Regulation** — Fully aligned with self ("This IS who I am")
6. **Intrinsic Motivation** — Pure enjoyment ("I love doing this")

**KEY FINDING:** #4-5 (Identified/Integrated) predict job performance BETTER than pure intrinsic motivation!

<speaker-notes>
This is the Van den Broeck (2021) meta-analysis finding that corrects Pink. Not all extrinsic motivation is bad—some forms are highly beneficial.

Low Autonomy End (Avoid): (1) Amotivation—complete lack of motivation, just showing up. (2) External Regulation—only motivated by external rewards or punishments, stops when pressure is removed. (3) Introjected Regulation—internal pressure from guilt, ego, or shame. "I should" but it doesn't feel authentic.

High Autonomy End (Cultivate): (4) Identified Regulation—you recognize the value even if it's not fun. "This matters to me." Example: Studying statistics isn't thrilling, but you value data literacy for your career. (5) Integrated Regulation—fully integrated into your identity. "This is who I am." Example: "I'm a continuous learner"—development is part of your self-concept. (6) Intrinsic Motivation—pure enjoyment and fascination. You'd do this work even without pay.

CRITICAL RESEARCH FINDING: Van den Broeck meta-analysis shows identified regulation predicts job performance BETTER than intrinsic motivation. Why? Because you can't be intrinsically motivated by everything at work. But you CAN find personal meaning in tasks you don't inherently enjoy. This is HUGE for career planning.

Connection to your PDP: Not every goal needs to be "fun"—some are valuable stepping stones. Ask "Does this align with my values?" (Identified) not just "Do I love this?" (Intrinsic). Week 5 Values clarification plus Week 12 values-career alignment cultivate identified regulation.

Example: You're not intrinsically motivated by networking events—they're awkward and tiring. BUT you identify with the value "building meaningful professional relationships." So you attend because it aligns with WHO YOU WANT TO BE—that's identified regulation, and it works.

The three psychological needs—autonomy, competence, relatedness—fuel this continuum.
</speaker-notes>
</slide>

# What Fuels Autonomous Motivation

**1. AUTONOMY**
- Choice and volition

**2. COMPETENCE**

<speaker-notes>
These aren't "nice to haves"—they're fundamental psychological needs. Like hunger or thirst, unmet needs cause problems. Met needs equal flourishing.

Deep dive on Autonomy: Misconception is "do whatever you want." Reality is "have meaningful choice within structure." Examples include choosing projects, setting your schedule, deciding methods. Career red flag is micromanagement, rigid rules, no input on decisions. Week 2 Context Engineering showed AI gives you MORE autonomy to focus on high-judgment work.

Week 4 Self-Managed Learning taught that monitoring progress builds competence.

Deep dive on Relatedness: Misconception is "having friends at work." Reality is "feeling connected, valued, part of something meaningful." Examples include supportive manager, collaborative team, shared mission. Career red flag is isolation, toxic culture, zero-sum competition. Connection to Assessment Handbook: Your Final Oral Exam assesses whether your PDP satisfies these needs. Examiners might ask "How does this goal build competence?" or "Where's autonomy in this plan?"

<slide number="16" layout="framework" title="Element 2: Serial Master">
# From T-Profile to M-Profile

**T-Profile (Single Expertise):**
Deep skill in one domain, broad general knowledge

**M-Profile (Multiple Expertises):**
Multiple deep skills, broad knowledge base

**Career Evolution:** Depth in one area → Add second expertise → Add third → Become uniquely valuable

<speaker-notes>
Early career: Develop ONE deep expertise to create a T-profile—accountant, designer, engineer. Mid-career: Add complementary expertise—accountant plus data analytics, designer plus UX research. Late career: M-profile makes you uniquely valuable and hard to replace.

Detailed explanation of T-Profile: Vertical bar represents deep expertise in one domain. Horizontal bar represents broad general knowledge. Example: You're a marketing specialist who knows deep digital marketing plus basic business knowledge.

M-Profile has multiple vertical bars representing multiple deep expertises. Example: Marketing specialist plus Data scientist plus Psychology researcher. This combination is RARE and valuable.

Why this matters: AI can replicate single expertise. AI struggles with COMBINATIONS of expertise requiring human judgment. Week 1-2 Future of Work emphasized multidisciplinary skills.

Connection to your PDP: 1-year goals develop primary expertise (your T). 3-year goals add complementary skill (start building second vertical). 5-year goals integrate multiple expertises (become M-profile).

Real example: Student 1-Year goal is "Build financial modeling expertise" (accounting T). Student 3-Year goal is "Add data science skills" (analytics vertical). Student 5-Year goal is "Lead financial AI implementation projects" (unique combination).

McKinsey insight: Serial mastery requires UNLEARNING old approaches. Week 4 Self-Managed Learning taught that metacognition helps you know when to unlearn.

<slide number="17" layout="framework" title="Element 3: Stretch">
# The Three Zones of Learning

**Comfort Zone (innermost circle)**
- Skills you've mastered
- Easy, automatic
- *Feels safe but no growth*

**Learning Zone (middle ring)**
- Challenging but achievable
- Requires effort and support
- *Optimal for growth* ← **OPERATE HERE**

**Panic Zone (outer ring)**
- Overwhelming, too difficult
- Causes stress and shutdown
- *Avoid or break down into smaller steps*

**Based on:** Yerkes-Dodson Law (1908)—moderate challenge = peak performance

<speaker-notes>
This is learning science 101—but most people don't apply it. Your PDP goals should be in Learning Zone, not Comfort or Panic.

Detailed explanation of Comfort Zone: Example is you're fluent in English, so taking English 101 would be comfort zone—waste of time. It's necessary for rest and recovery, but you can't stay here. Career danger is staying in comfort zone means skills become obsolete.

Learning Zone is the Goldilocks zone: Example is you know basic Python, take intermediate course—challenging but doable. Feels like productive struggle. Requires support such as mentor, tutor, study group. Week 11 SMART Goals emphasized "Achievable" but "Relevant" equals learning zone sweet spot.

Panic Zone: Example is never coded before, trying to build AI system from scratch—overwhelming. Physiological stress response activates (cortisol, fight-or-flight). Learning shuts down. Solution is break into smaller learning zone steps.

Connection to course: Week 8 Deep Work showed focused attention in learning zone builds expertise. Week 9 Growth Mindset taught viewing challenges as opportunities, not threats. Mentorship helps gauge what's learning zone versus panic zone for YOU.

Practical application for PDPs: Review each SMART goal—is it learning zone? Too easy means increase difficulty. Too hard means break into sub-goals or add support structures.

Self-assessment tool: Comfort is "I could do this in my sleep." Learning is "This will challenge me, but I can figure it out with effort." Panic is "This feels impossible, I don't even know where to start."

As you stretch and master new skills, you're building Element 4—your personal brand.

Proactivity implications: Don't wait for annual review to discuss growth. Seek feedback regularly (Week 7 EI). Identify learning opportunities in current role.

Risk management: Keep skills current to stay employable. Week 1 Future of Work showed 5-year skill half-life means continuous upskilling.

Example stories: Reactive old paradigm—"My company hasn't offered training, so I haven't learned anything new in 2 years." Gets laid off. Proactive new paradigm—"My company doesn't offer AI training, so I took online courses, built portfolio projects, joined AI community." Gets promoted or recruited.

**Ikigai (center):** The intersection of all four = sustainable, fulfilling career

**Partial Intersections (Warning Zones):**
- Love + Good At (no need/pay) = Hobby
- Love + Need + Pay (not good at) = Frustration
- Good At + Need + Pay (don't love) = Burnout

<speaker-notes>
Ikigai is a Japanese concept meaning "reason for being." This is THE framework for values-career alignment. Your PDP vision statement should reflect your ikigai or path toward it.

Deep dive on each circle: (1) What You LOVE (Passion)—Intrinsic motivation from SDT. What energizes you? Week 5 Holland Code identified interest-driven preferences. Warning: Passion alone doesn't pay bills.

(2) What You're GOOD AT (Competence)—Strengths, talents, developed skills. Week 5 VIA plus CliftonStrengths revealed natural abilities. Week 7 Transferable Skills showed cultivated competencies. Warning: Competence without passion equals golden handcuffs.

(3) What the World NEEDS (Market Demand)—Problems worth solving. Week 1-2 Future of Work identified AI era needs like judgment, creativity, empathy. Current trends include sustainability, healthcare, education, technology. Warning: Serving needs you don't care about equals empty work.

(4) What You Can Be PAID FOR (Economic Viability)—Salary, market rate, monetization. Reality check: Some passions are harder to monetize. Strategy is find adjacent career to fund passion, or niche where passion meets pay. Warning: Chasing money without meaning equals burnout.

The sweet spot at center is Ikigai—all four aligned. This is RARE immediately and often takes years to reach. Your 1-year goals might hit 2-3 circles. Your 5-year goals should aim for center.

Career stage application for Year 1-3 Recent Graduate: Often strong on 2-3 circles, weak on one. Common pattern is Good at plus Paid for, but not sure about Love or Need yet. Strategy: Explore through internships, side projects, mentorship.

Year 5-10 Mid-Career: Refining toward center. May need to pivot if stuck in 2-3 circle intersection. Strategy: Use Week 3 Reflection to assess fit, adjust course.

Year 10+ Established: Reached center or accepted trade-offs. Strategy: Mentor others, find meaning through impact.

Connection to your PDP Vision Statement exercise: Does it touch all four circles? Example: "I love [1] data storytelling, I'm good at [2] analytics and design, the world needs [3] evidence-based policy in Vietnam, and I can be paid [4] as a consultant or government analyst" equals Ikigai identified.

SMART Goals Alignment: Map each goal to ikigai circles. Goal "Complete data science bootcamp" develops circle 2 (Good At) and enables circle 4 (Paid For). Goal "Volunteer with education NGO" explores circle 3 (World Needs) and tests circle 1 (Love).

Red flags to identify: All goals in circle 4 (Paid For) only means money-driven with burnout risk. All goals in circle 1 (Love) only means passion without pragmatism with financial struggle risk. No circle 3 (World Needs) may feel meaningless long-term.

Practical tutorial activity: Draw your own ikigai diagram. Plot current job or studies—where do you sit now? Plot 5-year vision—where do you want to be? Identify gaps—which circles need development?

Critical thinking: Ikigai isn't static—circles shift as you grow. What you love at 22 doesn't equal what you love at 35. Use Week 3 Reflection annually to reassess. Personal mastery from Senge means continuously clarifying vision as circles evolve.

But even perfect ikigai won't work if you're burned out. Element 7 is Stay vital.
</speaker-notes>

# Wellness = Foundation for Everything

**Physical Vitality:**
- Sleep (7-9 hours)
- Exercise (150 min/week)
- Nutrition (fuel, not just food)

**Mental Vitality:**
- Stress management (Week 8: energy management)
- Mindfulness/reflection (Week 3: Gibbs/Kolb)
- Boundaries (work-life integration)

**Social Vitality:**
- Relationships (SDT: Relatedness need)
- Community (belonging)
- Support systems (mentorship, friends, family)

**Without vitality, all other elements collapse.**
*You can't learn, grow, or perform from a depleted state.*

<speaker-notes>
This is the LEAST sexy element—but the most critical. High achievers often sacrifice wellness for goals, leading to burnout and career derailment. Sustainable success requires vitality.

Deep dive on Physical Vitality Sleep: Research shows less than 7 hours equals cognitive impairment equivalent to intoxication. Affects learning, memory consolidation, emotional regulation. Week 8 Energy Management identified sleep as non-negotiable foundation. PDP application: How will you protect sleep during intense goal pursuit?

Exercise: WHO recommends 150 minutes per week moderate activity. Benefits include stress reduction, focus, energy, neurogenesis (brain growth). Doesn't have to be gym—walking, sports, yoga count. Connection to Deep Work from Week 8: Exercise improves concentration.

Nutrition: Brain is 2% body weight but uses 20% energy. Poor nutrition equals poor cognitive performance. Practical advice: Regular meals, hydration, minimize processed foods. Avoid using caffeine or energy drinks to compensate for poor sleep.

Deep dive on Mental Vitality Stress Management: Week 8 Schwartz showed energy, not time, is performance currency. Recovery rituals include breaks, hobbies, disconnecting. PDP risk: Ambitious goals without stress management equals burnout.

Mindfulness and Reflection: Week 3 Reflective Practice emphasized regular check-ins prevent drift. Senge Personal Mastery showed subconscious needs space to process. Practical: 10-minute daily reflection, weekly reviews, quarterly big-picture assessment.

Boundaries: "Work-life balance" is outdated—think "work-life integration." Set boundaries on availability, emails, working hours. Week 8 time-blocking includes personal time.

Deep dive on Social Vitality Relationships (Most Neglected): SDT Relatedness need emphasizes connection to others. Loneliness epidemic exists among young professionals. High-achieving students often sacrifice relationships for grades and regret it later. Quality beats quantity: Few deep relationships beat many superficial ones.

Community: Professional community from Week 7 networking and mentorship. Personal community includes friends, family, interest groups. Ikigai relatedness: Serving community fulfills "What world needs."

Support Systems: Mentorship Program builds support beyond this course. Peer networks: Classmates become lifelong professional connections. Family: Don't neglect foundational relationships.

The Vitality-Performance Cycle has two paths: Virtuous cycle (Stay Vital)—Good sleep leads to more energy, better performance, recognition, confidence, less stress, good sleep. Vicious cycle (Neglect Vitality)—Poor sleep leads to low energy, poor performance, stress, worse sleep, burnout.

Connection to your PDP Audit exercise: Review SMART goals—do ANY address wellness? If all goals are achievement-focused, add vitality goal. Example: "Exercise 3x per week for 30 minutes" (1-year goal). Example: "Maintain 7.5-hour sleep average" (ongoing habit).

Implementation section: PDP Part 5 (Accountability) should include wellness check-ins. Monthly review: Achievement progress AND vitality assessment. If vitality declining, pause, recover, then resume.

Real talk for students: You're young, you think you're invincible. "I'll sacrifice wellness now, rest later" equals future burnout. Sustainable careers are marathons, not sprints. Week 1 Five-year skill half-life means 40+ year career—MUST pace yourself.

Final Oral Exam consideration: Examiners may ask "How will you prevent burnout pursuing these ambitious goals?" If you have no answer, your plan is unrealistic.

Seven elements covered. Now let's synthesize 12 weeks of learning into the big picture.

# Your Commitment to Continuous Growth

**What happens after Week 12?**

The PDP you submit is NOT finished—it's a living document.

**Commit to:**
1. **Annual Review:** Every January, review and update your PDP
2. **Quarterly Check-Ins:** Every 3 months, assess progress
3. **Weekly Reflection:** Every Sunday, 15-minute check-in
4. **Lifelong Learning Habits:** Read, attend conferences, seek challenges, maintain mentorship

**Personal mastery isn't a course—it's a lifestyle.**

<speaker-notes>
This is the "so what?" moment. Students create PDPs for a grade—but the REAL value is making this a lifelong practice. Let's get specific about post-course commitment.

Deep dive on each commitment:

Annual Review (Strategic): When—Every January (New Year natural reflection point) OR your birthday. What to review: Update self-assessments (VIA, EI)—people change. Review 5-year goals: Still relevant? Adjust based on learning. Assess external changes: Industry shifts, economic conditions, AI advances. Celebrate wins: What did you achieve this year? Learn from failures: What didn't work? Why? Format: 2-3 hour retreat (coffee shop, park, quiet space). Tool: Week 3 Gibbs Cycle at annual scale. Output: Updated PDP Version 2.0, 3.0, etc.

Quarterly Check-Ins (Tactical): When—End of March, June, September, December. What to review: SMART goals on track for deadlines? Skills gap made progress? Accountability mechanisms working? Course corrections needed to adjust approach? Format: 1 hour focused review. Tool: OKR review from Week 11—score objectives, adjust key results. Output: Updated action items for next quarter.

Weekly Reflection (Operational): When—Sunday evening (prep for week) or Friday (close week). What to reflect: Wins this week (builds Week 9 growth mindset). Challenges (what did I learn?). One action for next week (continuous improvement). Format: 10-15 minutes journaling or voice memo. Tool: Week 3 Driscoll "What? So What? Now What?" Output: Clarity and focus for upcoming week.

Lifelong Learning Habits (Cultural): Reading—1 book per month equals 12 books per year equals 120 books per decade. Mix: 50% field-specific, 30% adjacent fields (serial mastery), 20% general wisdom. Week 4 Self-Managed Learning: Active reading with notes and application.

Conferences—1 per year minimum (industry conferences, academic symposia, workshops). Benefits: Network, trends, inspiration, visibility (personal brand). Budget: Invest in development (Week 12 Element 5: Own it).

Challenges—1 per quarter equals 4 per year of stretch goals (Week 12 Element 3). Examples: Learn new tool, present at event, lead project, mentor someone. Keeps you in learning zone, prevents stagnation.

Mentorship—Maintain relationship with mentors from this course. Seek new mentors as you advance (serial mentorship). BECOME a mentor (teaching solidifies learning).

Systems Thinking from Senge: These aren't isolated habits—they reinforce each other. Weekly reflection provides data for quarterly review, which informs annual strategy. Reading plus conferences plus challenges leads to skill development, which leads to goal achievement. It's a SYSTEM for continuous growth.

Practical commitment exercise for tutorial: "On a scale of 1-10, how committed are you to annual PDP reviews?" If less than 7, why? What's the barrier? How can you design accountability? Partner, calendar reminder, mentor check-in?

The meta-skill: This course taught you HOW to develop yourself. Most people never learn this—they drift. YOU now have frameworks, tools, and mindset. Using them is YOUR choice.
