# Week 1 Tutorial: Tutor Notes

## Timing Breakdown (90 minutes)

| Activity | Duration | Purpose |
|----------|----------|---------|
| Welcome & Quick Review | 15 min | Reinforce lecture frameworks, check comprehension |
| Main Activity: Individual Reflection Writing | 45 min | Deep personal reflection on career vision and skills |
| Pair-and-Share Discussion | 20 min | Peer learning, articulation practice |
| Wrap-up & Next Steps | 10 min | Administrative details, quiz/reading reminder |

---

## Valid Student Approaches

Students will approach this reflection task differently based on their career readiness and self-awareness. All five approaches below are valid and should be supported:

### 1. The Visionary
**Characteristics:** Has clear 2030 vision, identifies specific industry/role, articulates concrete goals
**Example:** "I want to be a sustainability consultant helping Vietnamese manufacturers transition to circular economy models. I'll need to develop systems thinking and stakeholder management skills."
**How to support:** Challenge them to identify potential obstacles and backup plans. Ask: "What if that industry changes dramatically? What transferable skills would still serve you?"

### 2. The Explorer
**Characteristics:** Uncertain about specific career path, focuses on broad skill development
**Example:** "I'm not sure if I want finance or marketing, but I know I want to work with data and people. I'll develop analytical and communication skills that work in both fields."
**How to support:** Validate that exploration is appropriate at this stage. Encourage them to identify what they want to learn more about through the mentorship program.

### 3. The Pragmatist
**Characteristics:** Focuses on employability, references specific job market data from lecture
**Example:** "The WEF report shows cybersecurity roles growing. I'll develop AI literacy and technical skills that employers are actively seeking."
**How to support:** Encourage them to also consider intrinsic motivation and values. Ask: "Beyond employability, what kind of work energizes you?"

### 4. The Values-Driven
**Characteristics:** Starts with personal values and impact, then identifies careers aligned with those
**Example:** "I value creativity and helping others. I want to use design thinking to solve community problems, maybe in social enterprise or NGO work."
**How to support:** Help them connect values to specific skills. Ask: "What skills do social entrepreneurs actually need? How can you develop those this semester?"

### 5. The Overwhelmed
**Characteristics:** Feels anxious about uncertainty, struggles to envision 2030, focuses on gaps rather than strengths
**Example:** "I don't know what I want yet. Everyone seems so far ahead. I'm not good at most of the WEF skills."
**How to support:** Normalize uncertainty. Emphasize that this is Week 1 of a 12-week journey. Help them identify ONE small strength to build from. Redirect from gaps to growth opportunities.

**Key message for all students:** This reflection is exploratory, not binding. The goal is to START thinking systematically, not to have everything figured out.

## Cultural Considerations (Vietnamese Students)

### 1. Family Expectations vs. Personal Desires
**Pattern:** Many students feel tension between their career interests and family expectations (e.g., family wants banking/medicine, student wants design/startups)

**How to facilitate:**
- Acknowledge this is a common tension globally, but particularly salient in Vietnamese culture
- Validate both family wisdom AND personal agency
- Frame as "exploring possibilities" rather than "defying parents"
- Suggest: "How might you honor your family's values while pursuing work that energizes you?"

### 2. Collectivist Framing of Success
**Pattern:** Students may define success in terms of group contribution rather than individual achievement

**How to facilitate:**
- Accept collectivist framing as valid (not all career models are individualistic)
- Ask: "What community or group do you want to contribute to? How does that shape your skill development?"
- Connect to Vietnamese context: "How does your vision contribute to Vietnam's development?"

### 3. Humility and Self-Promotion Discomfort
**Pattern:** Vietnamese students may downplay strengths or feel uncomfortable "bragging" in reflection

**How to facilitate:**
- Frame strengths identification as "evidence gathering" not self-promotion
- Use third-person exercise: "If a friend described your strengths, what would they say?"
- Emphasize: "Knowing your strengths isn't arrogance—it's career planning data"

### 4. Preference for Structured Guidance
**Pattern:** Students may want more specific instructions than the open-ended prompts provide

**How to facilitate:**
- Provide examples without prescribing content
- Offer sentence starters if students are stuck: "In 2030, I see myself..."
- Validate: "It's normal to feel uncertain with open-ended questions. That discomfort means you're thinking deeply."

### 5. English as Additional Language
**Pattern:** Some students may struggle with nuanced self-expression in English

**How to facilitate:**
- Allow Vietnamese language notes if that aids thinking, then translate key points
- Focus on ideas over grammar in this exploratory phase
- Provide vocabulary support: "What's another word for..."

## Common Misconceptions

### Misconception 1: "I need to pick one career path now and stick with it"
**Reality:** Modern careers are fluid. This reflection is a starting point, not a contract.
**Correction:** "This exercise helps you start thinking strategically. Your vision will evolve as you learn more about yourself and the market. That's expected and healthy."

### Misconception 2: "Technical skills are more important than soft skills"
**Reality:** WEF data shows cognitive, interpersonal, and self-management skills are equally critical.
**Correction:** "Notice that 7 of the top 10 WEF skills aren't technical. Employers need people who can think, collaborate, and adapt—technical skills alone aren't enough."

### Misconception 3: "AI will replace most jobs, so planning is pointless"
**Reality:** AI creates more jobs than it displaces, but requires different skills.
**Correction:** "The WEF projects net +78 million jobs by 2030. The challenge isn't job availability—it's developing the right skills. That's exactly what this course addresses."

### Misconception 4: "I should focus only on my weaknesses"
**Reality:** Career success often comes from leveraging strengths, not fixing all weaknesses.
**Correction:** "Identify gaps that block your vision, but also notice your strengths. Often, the best career strategy is to build on what you're already good at while addressing critical gaps."

### Misconception 5: "The 5-year vision needs to be realistic and achievable"
**Reality:** Visions can be aspirational—the planning process makes them achievable.
**Correction:** "Don't limit your vision to what seems 'realistic' now. Describe what you actually want, then work backward to identify the path. Ambitious visions drive ambitious development."

## Facilitation Tips

### During Quick Review (15 min)
- **Check comprehension with quick polls:** "Raise your hand if you remember the four pillars of career development"
- **Invite 2-3 students to share:** "Can someone explain Clark's 'Avoid Predictability' strategy in your own words?"
- **Address confusion immediately:** "What questions do you have about any of the frameworks before we apply them?"

### During Individual Writing (45 min)
- **Create focused environment:** "Minimize distractions. This is thinking time, not multitasking time."
- **Circulate and check progress:** After 20 minutes, check that students are writing, not stuck
- **Offer scaffolding for stuck students:**
  - "Start with Part 1 (vision) or Part 2 (skills)—whichever feels easier"
  - "Write in bullets first, then expand to paragraphs"
  - "If you're stuck on 2030, describe what you DON'T want—then flip it"
- **Manage time:** Give 20-minute, 10-minute, and 5-minute warnings

### During Pair-and-Share (20 min)
- **Model good pairing behavior:** Before students pair up, demonstrate active listening and constructive feedback with a volunteer
- **Facilitate pairing:** Mix students who don't know each other well (builds network)
- **Monitor discussions:** Circulate to ensure both partners are engaging
- **Intervene if needed:** If one partner dominates or pairs are off-topic, gently redirect
- **Debrief with whole group:** "What surprised you in your partner's vision?" "What feedback did you receive that was helpful?"

### During Wrap-up (10 min)
- **Emphasize saving work:** "You will need this reflection in Week 11. Save it now."
- **Preview Week 2:** "Next week we dive deeper into specific AI-era skills. The mentorship program launches—come ready to articulate what you want to develop."
- **Reminder about quiz:** "Quiz is due before next lecture. It tests framework knowledge—review your lecture notes."
- **Invite questions:** "What's unclear about the quiz, readings, or mentorship program?"

## Common Questions & Suggested Responses

**Q: "What if I don't know what I want to do in 5 years?"**
A: "That's completely normal. This exercise helps you START exploring, not finalize decisions. Describe what interests you NOW, knowing it may change. The process of thinking systematically is the goal."

**Q: "Can I write in Vietnamese and translate later?"**
A: "For today's in-class reflection, yes—capture your ideas in whatever language helps you think. For the formal PDP submission in Week 11, it needs to be in English, but this is just exploration."

**Q: "How long should my reflection be?"**
A: "300-400 words total across all three parts. Quality over quantity—be specific and honest rather than filling space."

**Q: "Will you grade this reflection?"**
A: "No, this is ungraded formative work. It's for YOUR benefit—to start the thinking process that feeds into your PDP later. Be honest; there's no 'wrong' answer."

**Q: "What if my skills are all gaps? I don't have strengths yet."**
A: "Everyone has strengths, even if you don't recognize them yet. Think about what you're naturally drawn to, what friends ask you for help with, what courses you enjoyed. Those are clues to strengths."

**Q: "Do I need to pick a specific job title for my 2030 vision?"**
A: "You can, but it's not required. It's equally valid to describe the type of work, the impact you want to have, or the skills you want to use, even if you don't know the exact job title."

**Q: "What if the one skill I choose isn't on the WEF list?"**
A: "That's fine! The WEF list provides guidance, not limits. If there's a skill critical to YOUR vision, choose that. Just explain why it matters for your goals."

1. **Digital copy of lecture slides** (particularly slides on WEF skills and Clark's five strategies) for students who want to reference them
2. **Example reflection** (anonymized from previous student or created by you) to show what good work looks like—BUT don't share until students have started writing (avoid template-copying)

   - "My current strengths include..."
   - "I need to develop..."
   - "This semester, I commit to..."
4. **Pairing protocol** (printed or projected) to guide pair-and-share structure
5. **Week 2 preview slide** with reading list and mentorship program overview

## Adaptation for Different Class Sizes

**Small class (<20 students):**
- Do whole-group sharing instead of pairs
- Each student shares 1-minute highlight from their reflection
- Create more interactive review session with discussion

**Large class (>40 students):**
- Use breakout groups of 3-4 for sharing instead of pairs
- Provide more structured handouts with sentence starters
- Consider using online collaboration tools (Padlet, Miro) for sharing insights

**Mixed ability:**
- Offer advanced challenge: "For those who already have clarity, also identify potential disruptors to your plan and how you'd adapt"
- Provide scaffolding: Sentence starters, more examples, one-on-one check-ins during writing time

---

## Success Indicators

By the end of this tutorial, students should:
- ✅ Have completed a 300-400 word reflection saved for future reference
- ✅ Be able to name their 2030 vision in 1-2 sentences
- ✅ Have identified at least one skill to develop this semester
- ✅ Understand how this reflection connects to the PDP assessment in Week 11
- ✅ Feel less anxious about career uncertainty (or have normalized the anxiety)
- ✅ Know what to do before next class (quiz, reading, mentorship prep)

**If students are leaving confused or anxious:** Revisit the key message: "This is Week 1 of 12. You're not supposed to have everything figured out. You're starting a process of systematic self-discovery. Each week builds on the previous."
