# Week 8 Tutorial: Tutor Facilitation Guide

**Course:** PPDK66B Personal & Professional Development
**Tutorial Topic:** Personal Productivity System Design
**Duration:** 90 minutes
**Difficulty Level:** Moderate (requires self-awareness and honest self-assessment from students)

---

## Tutorial Overview

**Learning Objectives:**
By the end of this tutorial, students will be able to:
1. Assess personal energy patterns across four dimensions and match tasks to energy levels
2. Analyze current time allocation using Eisenhower Matrix and redesign schedule for increased Quadrant 2 focus
3. Design time-blocked schedules that protect deep work and recovery
4. Create habit stacks using Clear's Four Laws and implementation intentions
5. Design targeted recovery rituals at multiple timescales based on individual energy bottlenecks

**Key Challenge:**
This tutorial requires students to be brutally honest about how they currently spend time and energy. Many will discover they waste significant time (Q4) and neglect important long-term activities (Q2). Your role is to:
- Create safe space for honest self-assessment (not judgment)
- Help students design realistic systems (not overly ambitious plans that fail)
- Emphasize iteration over perfection (systems evolve through testing)
- Connect every activity to PDP assessment (motivate engagement)

**Success Indicators:**
- Students complete energy audit with honest ratings (not idealized)
- Students' time-blocked schedules include recovery and are realistic (not 12-hour study marathons)
- Students design habit stacks that leverage existing routines (not fantasy morning routines)
- Class discussion reveals understanding that systems beat willpower
- Students leave with specific commitment to test ONE system this week

### Activity 1: Energy Audit & Mapping (20 minutes)

**Introduction (1 min):**
- "We're starting with energy audit because everything else depends on this"
- "If you don't know WHEN you have peak energy, you can't design effective schedules"
- "This is highly individual—your patterns won't match your roommate's"

#### Part 1: Four-Dimension Energy Assessment (8 min)

**Demonstration (2 min):**
Project energy audit template and fill out ONE time block as example:

```
Time Block: 9am-12pm (Late Morning)
Physical Energy: 4 (high—rested, fed, energized)
Emotional Energy: 3 (moderate—some stress about upcoming deadline)
Mental Energy: 5 (peak—this is my best thinking time)
Spiritual Energy: 4 (high—feeling purposeful about today's work)
Notes: "Attend class 9-11am, then study session 11am-12pm"
```

- Point out: "Different dimensions can have different levels at same time"
- "This is normal—mental energy can be high while emotional energy is low"

**Student Work Time (6 min):**
- "Now complete the template for YOUR typical weekday"
- "Be honest—this is for you, not graded"
- "If you're not sure, estimate based on how you feel during these times"

**Tutor Circulation Tips:**
- Look for students rating everything 3 (avoiding honest assessment)
  - Coach: "Think about this morning—was your energy truly 'moderate' at 6am or was it lower?"
- Look for students who don't see variation (all ratings same throughout day)
  - Coach: "Most people have peaks and valleys—when do you feel most alert?"
- Look for students who can't remember typical patterns
  - Coach: "Think about yesterday specifically, or last Tuesday"

**Common Student Struggles:**

**Struggle 1: Confusing dimensions**
- Example: Rating physical low but actually describing mental fatigue
- Coach: "Physical = body (tired, energized). Mental = brain (can/can't focus). Are you physically tired or mentally foggy?"

**Struggle 2: Idealized ratings (what they wish) vs. actual**
- Coach: "Don't rate what you think SHOULD be—rate what actually IS"
- Example: Student rates morning energy 5 because they "should" be morning person, but they're actually groggy

**Struggle 3: Missing emotional/spiritual dimensions**
- Many students focus only on physical/mental
- Coach: "Emotional = your mood, stress level, anxiety. Spiritual = feeling of purpose and meaning. How are those?"

#### Part 2: Pattern Analysis (7 min)

**Introduction (1 min):**
- "Now we're mining your data for insights"
- "The audit is just numbers—patterns reveal your strategy"

**Student Work Time (6 min):**
- "Answer the pattern questions for each dimension"
- "Identify your LOWEST dimension overall—that's your bottleneck"

**Tutor Circulation Tips:**
- Help students who struggle to see patterns
  - Point to their table: "I see your mental energy is 5 at 8am and 2 at 9pm—what does that tell you?"
- Celebrate insights: "That's a great observation—you've identified your peak window"
- For students with flat patterns (no variation):
  - Coach: "That's unusual—most people have clear peaks/valleys. Let's look at one day in detail. How did you feel hour-by-hour yesterday?"

**Common Insights Students Discover:**
- "My mental energy peaks early morning but I waste it on easy tasks"
- "I try to study at night when my energy is depleted—no wonder it doesn't work"
- "My emotional energy is consistently low—I need recovery strategies"

#### Part 3: Strategic Implications (5 min)

**Facilitating This Section (2 min):**
- "Turn your patterns into scheduling principles"
- "This is the 'so what?'—if mental energy peaks 8-10am, WHAT should you schedule then?"

**Student Work Time (3 min):**
- "Answer the four strategic questions"
- "Be specific—don't just say 'morning is best'; say what time range"

**Pair Check-In (Facilitate) (2 min per person):**
- "Share your peak performance window and energy bottleneck with partner"
- "Partner's job: Ask 'What's one change you could make based on this?'"

**Listen for Quality Insights:**
- Good: "I should schedule deep work 8-10am, not 9pm when I'm depleted"
- Weak: "I should manage my energy better" (too vague)
- Coach weak responses toward specificity

**Key Teaching Point (1 min):**
- "What you've just done—energy audit—most people never do"
- "They fight their biology instead of working with it"
- "You now have data to design systems that actually work for YOU"

### Activity 2: Eisenhower Matrix Mapping & Time-Blocking (25 minutes)

**Introduction (2 min):**
- "Now we're analyzing HOW you currently spend time"
- "Most students are shocked to discover how little time goes to important long-term activities"
- "We'll then redesign one day using time-blocking to protect what matters"

#### Part 1: Current Time Allocation Analysis (8 min)

**Demonstration (2 min):**
Show example of student's day categorized into quadrants:

```
Q1 (Urgent + Important) - 7 hours:
- Attended classes (4h)
- Studied for tomorrow's quiz (2h)
- Group presentation prep for tomorrow (1h)

Q2 (Not Urgent + Important) - 1 hour:
- Gym (30 min)
- Reviewed long-term PDP goals (30 min)

Q3 (Urgent + Not Important) - 2 hours:
- Low-priority group chat responses (30 min)
- Unimportant club meeting (1h)
- Helped roommate with non-urgent task (30 min)

Q4 (Not Urgent + Not Important) - 3 hours:
- Scrolled social media (1.5h)
- Watched YouTube (1h)
- Mobile games (30 min)
```

- Ask class: "What do you notice?"
- Desired observation: "Only 1 hour in Q2—the most important quadrant!"
- "This is typical—most students spend 5-10% in Q2, not the 60-65% of high performers"

**Student Work Time (6 min):**
- "List everything you did last Tuesday (or recent typical day)"
- "Categorize into quadrants"
- "Calculate hours per quadrant"
- "Be honest about time wasters (Q4)—we all have them"

**Tutor Circulation Tips:**

**Challenge 1: Students can't remember their day**
- Solution: "Think about most recent weekday—even if it was unusual, it's data"
- Or: "Describe your 'typical' routine even if you're estimating"

**Challenge 2: Students defensive about Q4 time**
- Normalize: "Everyone has Q4 time—I spent 2 hours on YouTube last week"
- Reframe: "We're not judging; we're identifying opportunity for reclaiming time"

**Challenge 3: Students mis-categorize**
- Common error: Classifying all classes as Q1 (urgent + important)
  - Some classes are Q1 (required, exam soon), but many are Q2 (important for learning, not immediately urgent)
- Common error: Saying all social media is Q4
  - Some is Q4 (mindless scrolling), but messaging friend for emotional support might be Q2 (important relationship maintenance)

**Discussion Prompt (After individual work):**
- "By show of hands: How many of you spent less than 2 hours in Q2 yesterday?"
- Expect many hands—this normalizes the problem
- "This is the productivity trap—we're busy (Q1 + Q3) but not investing in what matters (Q2)"

#### Part 2: Time-Blocking a Redesigned Day (12 min)

**Demonstration (4 min):**
Project annotated time-blocked schedule and explain design choices:

```
7:00-9:00am → DEEP WORK (Q2) [Peak mental energy from energy audit]
  WHY: Hardest cognitive work during peak capacity
  WHAT: Most challenging subject study, phone off, library

9:00-9:15am → Break [Micro-recovery after intense focus]
  WHY: Mental energy needs renewal after 2 hours

9:15-11:00am → Class (Q1) [Required attendance]
  WHY: Important and scheduled

12:00-1:00pm → Lunch + Social [Low energy post-lunch dip]
  WHY: Don't fight biology; use low-energy time for rest/connection

2:00-4:00pm → DEEP WORK (Q2) [Second energy peak]
  WHY: Creative/writing work fits afternoon energy

4:00-5:00pm → Skill Building (Q2) [Moderate energy]
  WHY: Important but not urgent—this is Q2 investment

5:00-6:00pm → Exercise (Q2) [Physical recovery]
  WHY: Builds capacity, restores energy for evening
```

- Point out: "Only ~4 hours deep work—not 8-10. That's realistic."
- Point out: "Recovery isn't afterthought—it's scheduled (breaks, lunch, exercise)"
- Point out: "Q2 activities protected like appointments"

**Student Work Time (8 min):**
- "Design YOUR time-blocked day using YOUR energy audit"
- "Match task types to energy levels"
- "Include recovery—it's non-negotiable"
- "Be realistic about deep work hours (2-4, not 8-10)"

**Tutor Circulation Tips:**

**Red Flag 1: Overscheduling deep work**
- Student schedules 8+ hours of deep work
- Coach: "Research shows 4 hours max sustainable daily. What can you move to 'moderate work' category?"

**Red Flag 2: No recovery scheduled**
- Student has back-to-back work blocks with no breaks
- Coach: "Where are your breaks? Even machines need downtime. Add 10-15 min recovery after each 50-90 min work block."

**Red Flag 3: Fighting energy patterns**
- Student schedules hardest work during their low-energy time
- Coach: "I see your energy audit shows low mental energy 9pm. Why schedule complex studying then? What about your 8am peak?"

**Red Flag 4: No buffer time**
- Student accounts for all 16 waking hours
- Coach: "What happens when friend needs help or you get sick? Build in 20-30% buffer—plan for only 60-70% of day."

**Green Flags (Celebrate These):**
- Student matches energy to tasks: "Great—you're scheduling deep work during your mental energy peak!"
- Student includes recovery: "I love that you scheduled walk breaks—that's how you sustain performance"
- Student is realistic: "This is a sustainable schedule—you can maintain this long-term"

#### Part 3: One-Week Implementation Plan (5 min)

**Facilitating This Section:**
- "Plans without action are fantasies"
- "Let's make this real—when THIS WEEK will you test your time-blocked schedule?"

**Student Work Time (3 min):**
- "Answer the four implementation questions"
- "Choose one specific day to test (not 'sometime this week')"

**Pair Check-In (2 min per person):**
- "Share your time-blocked schedule with partner"
- "Partner reviews for: Energy matching? Recovery included? Realistic deep work hours?"

**Class Discussion (2 min):**
- "What day this week will you test time-blocking?" (Get specific commitments)
- "What's your biggest anticipated obstacle?" (Prepare for common barriers)
- Expected obstacles: Roommate interruptions, group chat distractions, unexpected class assignments
- For each, ask class: "How could you mitigate that?"

**Key Teaching Point:**
- "Time-blocking will fail sometimes—that's okay"
- "When unexpected task arises, don't abandon structure—REVISE"
- "Quickly adjust: 'This takes 1 hour; what block do I shorten or move?'"

### Activity 3: Habit Stacking Design (25 minutes)

**Introduction (2 min):**
- "Habits are leverage—good systems reduce decision fatigue"
- "Habit stacking is the simplest, most effective technique"
- "You'll design habits that support your PDP goals using Clear's Four Laws"

#### Part 1: Identify Target Habits (7 min)

**Demonstration (2 min):**
Share example of goal → habits analysis:

```
PDP Goal: Improve data analytics skills for consulting career

Supporting Habits (if done daily for 6 months):
1. Daily 15-min Python/R practice problems
   Impact: Build coding fluency, pattern recognition, confidence

2. Daily reading of analytics case studies (10 min)
   Impact: Learn real-world applications, business context

3. Weekly participation in analytics study group
   Impact: Peer learning, accountability, network building
```

- Point out: "Habits are small, daily actions. They're not 'become data analyst'—they're specific behaviors."

**Student Work Time (5 min):**
- "Review your PDP goals"
- "List 3-5 habits that would support these goals if done consistently"
- "Select 2-3 most important"
- "For each, write impact statement: 'If I did this daily for 6 months...'"

**Tutor Circulation Tips:**

**Issue 1: Habits too big**
- Student writes: "Study analytics 3 hours daily"
- Coach: "That's too big to sustain. What's the minimal version? Maybe '15-min practice problems'?"

**Issue 2: Habits too vague**
- Student writes: "Improve English"
- Coach: "That's a goal, not a habit. What specific behavior? 'Read 10 pages English book'? 'Listen to 15-min podcast'?"

**Issue 3: Habits not aligned with PDP**
- Student chooses habits they think they "should" do vs. habits that support actual goals
- Coach: "How does this habit advance your specific PDP goals? If it doesn't, choose different habit."

**Issue 4: Student has no PDP goals yet**
- Solution: "What career/skill are you working toward? Pick habits that support that direction."

#### Part 2: Apply Four Laws to Each Habit (10 min)

**Demonstration (3 min):**
Walk through Four Laws applied to sample habit:

```
Habit: Daily 15-minute English podcast listening

1st Law: Make It Obvious
- Implementation intention: "I will listen to podcast at 7:30am in kitchen"
- Habit stack: "After I pour morning coffee, I will start podcast"
- Environment design: Earbuds on counter next to coffee maker

2nd Law: Make It Attractive
- Temptation bundling: "I only drink favorite coffee while listening"
- Social motivation: "Share insights with study group weekly"

3rd Law: Make It Easy
- Two-Minute Rule: "Just start episode" (don't commit to finishing)
- Reduce friction: "App open to queued episode night before"

4th Law: Make It Satisfying
- Immediate reward: "Coffee enjoyment paired with learning"
- Habit tracking: "Check box on calendar; aim for 30-day streak"
```

- Emphasize: "1st Law (Obvious) is most critical—most habits fail because you forget"

**Student Work Time (7 min):**
- "Take your 2-3 habits"
- "For each, complete Four Laws template"
- "Be specific—vague plans fail"

**Tutor Circulation Tips:**

**Common Mistake 1: Vague 1st Law (Obvious)**
- Student writes: "I will practice when I wake up"
- Coach: "What time exactly? Where? Trigger is too vague. Try: 'After I brush teeth, I will practice at my desk'"

**Common Mistake 2: No 2nd Law (Attractive)**
- Student leaves this blank or writes "It's good for me"
- Coach: "That's not attractive in the moment. Can you pair it with something enjoyable? Coffee? Music? Social accountability?"

**Common Mistake 3: Habit too big for 3rd Law (Easy)**
- Student's habit: "Study 2 hours daily"
- Coach: "That's not easy—it's hard. What's the 2-minute version to start? 'Open textbook and read one page'?"

**Common Mistake 4: Delayed reward in 4th Law**
- Student writes: "Reward is getting better over time"
- Coach: "That's too delayed. What feels good RIGHT AFTER you complete the habit? Checking a box? Treating yourself to 10-min YouTube?"

**Celebrate Good Applications:**
- "Excellent habit stack—'After X, I will Y' is perfect formula"
- "I love that you paired it with coffee—that makes it attractive"
- "Great two-minute start—very easy to begin, often leads to continuing"

#### Part 3: Build a Habit Stack Chain (8 min)

**Demonstration (2 min):**
Show morning routine habit stack:

```
After my alarm goes off, I will sit up immediately.
After I sit up, I will drink water on nightstand.
After I drink water, I will make my bed.
After I make my bed, I will do 10 push-ups.
After push-ups, I will shower.
After shower, I will review my time-block schedule.
```

- Count habits: "Six habits stacked into one automatic routine"
- Timeline: "After 3-4 weeks, alarm → entire routine (no thinking required)"

**Student Work Time (6 min):**
- "Choose one routine: morning, evening study, pre-bed, post-class"
- "Build 3-5 habit chain using formula"
- "Make sure habits flow logically"

**Tutor Circulation Tips:**

**Issue 1: Unreliable anchor**
- Student anchors to "when I feel ready"
- Coach: "That's not reliable—it varies. Pick something you do daily automatically. Alarm? Sitting at desk? Finishing dinner?"

**Issue 2: Habits don't flow logically**
- Student: "After breakfast, I will exercise. After exercise, I will eat breakfast."
- Coach: "Wait—you can't eat breakfast after exercising if breakfast is the trigger. Reorder: After wake up, exercise. After exercise, eat breakfast."

**Issue 3: Chain too long**
- Student lists 10+ habits
- Coach: "Start with 3-5. You can expand later. Too many = overwhelming = failure."

**Pair Check-In (Facilitate):**
- "Share habit chain with partner"
- "Partner evaluates: Reliable anchor? Logical flow? Small enough to sustain?"

**Key Teaching Point:**
- "Habit chains work because the first habit triggers the second, which triggers the third"
- "After automation, you don't decide to do the routine—alarm triggers it automatically"
- "This preserves willpower for truly difficult decisions"

## Common Student Questions & Answers

**Q: "What if I can't stick to my time-blocked schedule?"**
A: "Schedules will fail—that's normal. When unexpected task arises, revise the schedule (don't abandon it). Ask: 'What block do I move or shorten?' Iteration is the process."

**Q: "How do I time-block when my schedule changes daily?"**
A: "Block your variable schedule. If you don't know specifics, block categories: 'Class/meetings 9-12', 'Deep work 2-4', etc. Flex within categories."

**Q: "What if my energy is always low (no peaks)?"**
A: "That's a red flag for burnout or health issue. Prioritize recovery immediately. Also: Your peaks might be subtle—compare 8am to 8pm. Which is less depleted?"

**Q: "I tried habit stacking before and failed. Why will it work now?"**
A: "Most failures are 1st Law (Obvious) problems—you forgot. This time: Anchor to reliable existing habit, make cue impossible to miss (visual reminder), start ridiculously small (2-min version)."

**Q: "Is it okay to skip recovery when busy with exams?"**
A: "No—that's when you need it MOST. Performance degrades without recovery. Strategic 10-min breaks during exam prep actually improve retention over marathon cramming."

**Q: "These systems feel selfish (family expects my time)"**
A: "Reframe: Family time can be emotional energy recovery. Time-blocking protects BOTH study time AND family time. You're not saying no to family—you're saying yes to both, strategically."

**Q: "What if I'm not a 'habit person'?"**
A: "You already have habits—brushing teeth, eating breakfast, checking phone. We're just deliberately designing new ones. Everyone can build habits; it's neurological, not personality."

**Q: "How many habits should I stack?"**
A: "Start with ONE habit stack (3-5 habits in the chain). After it's automatic (4-6 weeks), add another. Don't build 10 stacks simultaneously—recipe for failure."

## Troubleshooting Guide

**Issue:** Student completes energy audit but all ratings are 3 (avoiding self-assessment)
**Solution:** Ask about specific recent moments: "Yesterday at 8am, were you energized or groggy? Rate that specifically." Concrete examples override abstract reluctance.

**Issue:** Student designs overly ambitious system (12-hour study days, 10 new habits)
**Solution:** Celebrate ambition, then reality-check: "This shows great goals. But if this schedule failed within 2 days, would you be discouraged? Let's design version you can sustain 6 months, then expand."

**Issue:** Student resistant to scheduling recovery ("That's wasted time")

**Issue:** Student says they have no time for anything (class + family obligations fill entire day)
**Solution:** Time audit their Q4 time. Most students have 2-4 hours daily of hidden time wasters. Reclaim 1 hour of Q4 → 7 hours/week for Q2.

**Issue:** Student frustrated that systems seem complicated
**Solution:** Validate frustration, then reframe: "Yes, designing takes 2 hours today. But this saves 10+ hours/week of wasted effort. Front-load the thinking."

**Issue:** Pair check-ins superficial (partners just saying "looks good")
**Solution:** Give specific evaluation prompts: "Partner: Check if deep work is during peak energy. Check if recovery is scheduled. Check if habits use reliable anchor."
