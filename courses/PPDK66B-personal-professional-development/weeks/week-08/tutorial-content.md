## Quick Review

**Key concepts from today's lecture:**
- **Energy Management:** Managing renewable energy (physical, emotional, mental, spiritual) rather than finite time
- **Eisenhower Matrix:** Prioritizing by Urgency vs. Importance—high performers invest in Quadrant 2 (Important but Not Urgent)
- **Deep Work:** High-Quality Work = Time × Intensity of Focus—distraction-free concentration creates premium value
- **Habit Formation:** Cue-Routine-Reward loop; habits take ~66 days (18-254 range); Four Laws (Obvious, Attractive, Easy, Satisfying)
- **Habit Stacking:** "After [CURRENT HABIT], I will [NEW HABIT]" leverages existing routines
- **Recovery Rituals:** Micro, daily, weekly, semester-level renewals enable sustainable high performance

**What good work looks like:**
- Time-blocked schedules that match high-energy periods with demanding work
- Specific habit stacks using implementation intentions formula
- Energy management plans across all four dimensions with scheduled recovery
- Systems that are realistic and sustainable (not overly ambitious plans that fail by week 2)

## Activity 1: Energy Audit & Mapping (20 minutes)

**Assessment Connection:** Understanding your energy patterns is essential for your PDP implementation strategies. If you schedule deep work on your most challenging goal during your lowest-energy time of day, you'll fail. This audit reveals when to schedule what types of work in your 1-year, 3-year, and 5-year plans.

**The Challenge:**
You'll map your actual energy levels across a typical weekday, identifying patterns across Schwartz's four dimensions. This self-awareness enables strategic scheduling.

**Your Turn:**

### Part 3: Strategic Implications (5 min)

**Your Task:**
Based on your energy audit, identify strategic scheduling principles for YOUR productivity system.

**Questions to Answer:**

**1. Peak Performance Window:**
- When is your mental energy consistently highest?
- **Implication:** Schedule deep work / most challenging tasks during this window
- Example: "Mental energy peaks 8-10am → Schedule complex studying then, not at 9pm"

**2. Energy Troughs:**
- When is your energy lowest across most dimensions?
- **Implication:** Schedule low-demand activities (admin, errands, rest) during troughs
- Example: "All energy low 12-1pm (post-lunch dip) → Don't fight it, take lunch + walk break"

**3. Energy Bottleneck:**
- Which dimension is consistently lowest?
- **Implication:** This needs active recovery strategy
- Example: "Emotional energy consistently low (family pressure, academic stress) → Need daily emotional recovery ritual"

**4. Natural Rhythms:**
- Do you have a second energy peak in afternoon/evening?
- **Implication:** Can schedule second deep work block, or reserve for creative work
- Example: "Second mental peak 3-5pm → Good for writing, presentations, creative projects"

**Your Turn:**
1. Write your peak performance window
2. Identify your main energy trough
3. Note your energy bottleneck dimension
4. List any other patterns you noticed

**Pair Check-In (2 min per person):**
- Share your peak performance window with partner
- Share your energy bottleneck
- Partner asks: "What's one change you could make based on this insight?"

**Key Takeaway:**
Your energy patterns are NOT the same as everyone else's. Generic productivity advice ("wake at 5am!") fails because it ignores individual patterns. Design YOUR system around YOUR energy.

## Activity 2: Eisenhower Matrix Mapping & Time-Blocking (25 minutes)

**Assessment Connection:** Your PDP requires realistic implementation timelines. If you can't identify when you'll actually work on your goals, they won't happen. This activity teaches you to protect time for Important but Not Urgent activities (Quadrant 2)—exactly where goal-achievement work lives.

**The Challenge:**
You'll analyze how you currently spend time using the Eisenhower Matrix, then redesign one day using time-blocking to increase Quadrant 2 focus.

### Part 1: Current Time Allocation Analysis (8 min)

**Your Task:**
Categorize your activities from a recent typical weekday into the four Eisenhower quadrants.

**The Matrix:**

|  | **URGENT** | **NOT URGENT** |
|---|---|---|
| **IMPORTANT** | **Q1: Crises/Deadlines**<br>DO FIRST | **Q2: Growth/Prevention**<br>SCHEDULE & PROTECT |
| **NOT IMPORTANT** | **Q3: Interruptions**<br>MINIMIZE | **Q4: Time Wasters**<br>ELIMINATE |

**Examples for Students:**

**Q1 (Urgent + Important):**
- Exam tomorrow, assignment due today
- Required class attendance
- Sick family member needs help
- Emergency group meeting for presentation today

**Q2 (Not Urgent + Important):**
- Starting major assignment when first assigned (not night before)
- Building relationship with professor/mentor
- Skill development for future career
- Exercise, sleep, health maintenance
- Strategic planning and reflection
- Deep work on long-term projects

**Q3 (Urgent + Not Important):**
- Unimportant meeting that could be email
- Responding to every group chat message immediately
- Others' low-priority requests
- Some social obligations with low personal value

**Q4 (Not Urgent + Not Important):**
- Mindless social media scrolling
- Excessive TV/YouTube
- Busywork that feels productive but doesn't advance goals

**Your Turn:**
1. List everything you did last Tuesday (or typical recent weekday)
2. Estimate hours spent on each activity
3. Categorize each into Q1, Q2, Q3, or Q4
4. Calculate total hours per quadrant

**Example Analysis:**
```
Q1 (7 hours): Attended classes (4h), studied for quiz next day (2h), group meeting for tomorrow's presentation (1h)
Q2 (1 hour): Went to gym (30min), reviewed long-term PDP goals (30min)
Q3 (2 hours): Responded to low-priority group chat (30min), attended club meeting that wasn't valuable (1h), helped roommate with non-urgent task (30min)
Q4 (3 hours): Scrolled Instagram (1h), watched YouTube (1.5h), played mobile games (30min)
Sleep/Personal: 8 hours
Meals/Hygiene: 2 hours
TOTAL: 23 hours accounted for
```

**Reflection Questions:**
1. What percentage of your time is in Q2 (Important but Not Urgent)?
   - High performers: 60-65%
   - Most students: 5-10%
2. How much time is in Q4 (Not Important, Not Urgent)?
   - This is "hidden time" you could reclaim
3. Is Q1 large because you neglected Q2 earlier?
   - Example: Assignment is Q1 crisis today because you didn't start it in Q2 last week

### Part 2: Time-Blocking a Redesigned Day (12 min)

**Your Task:**
Redesign one typical weekday using time-blocking, dramatically increasing Q2 time.

**Time-Blocking Principles:**
1. **Block every hour** (including breaks, meals, sleep)
2. **Protect deep work blocks** like doctor appointments (Q2 activities)
3. **Batch similar tasks** (emails, admin, errands)
4. **Match energy to task type:**
   - Peak mental energy → Most demanding cognitive work (Q2 deep work)
   - Moderate energy → Classes, meetings, moderate tasks
   - Low energy → Admin, breaks, recovery
5. **Build in buffer:** Plan for only 60-70% of day; rest is flex time

**Template:**

| Time | Energy Level | Block Type | Specific Activity | Quadrant | Notes |
|------|-------------|------------|------------------|----------|-------|
| 6:00-7:00 | Low | Morning Routine | Wake, breakfast, prepare | - | Build in buffer |
| 7:00-9:00 | Peak Mental | **DEEP WORK** | Most challenging subject study | Q2 | Phone off, library |
| 9:00-9:15 | - | Break | Walk outside | - | Movement recovery |
| 9:15-11:00 | High | Class | Attend lecture | Q1/Q2 | Active engagement |
| 11:00-12:00 | Moderate | Moderate Work | Group project work | Q1 | Collaborative |
| 12:00-1:00 | Low (post-lunch) | Lunch + Social | Eat with friends | - | Emotional recovery |
| 1:00-2:00 | Low-Moderate | Shallow Work | Emails, organization, admin | Q3 | Batch low-priority |
| 2:00-2:15 | - | Break | Stretch, snack | - | Physical recovery |
| 2:15-4:00 | Moderate-High | **DEEP WORK** | Creative/writing work | Q2 | Second focus block |
| 4:00-5:00 | Moderate | Skill Building | Language practice / Career development | Q2 | Important, not urgent |
| 5:00-6:00 | Moderate | Exercise | Gym / Sport / Movement | Q2 | Physical recovery |
| 6:00-7:00 | Low | Dinner + Family | Eat, family time | - | Emotional recovery |
| 7:00-8:00 | Low-Moderate | Light Study | Review notes, flashcards | Q2 | Low cognitive demand |
| 8:00-9:00 | Low | Social / Hobby | Relax, socialize, hobby | - | Spiritual recovery |
| 9:00-10:00 | Very Low | Wind-Down | Tech-free, prepare for bed | - | Sleep prep |
| 10:00-6:00 | - | Sleep | 8 hours | - | Physical recovery |

**Key Features of This Schedule:**
- **Q2 deep work protected:** 7-9am and 2:15-4pm (total 3.75 hours of highest-value work)
- **Energy-matched:** Hardest work during peak mental energy
- **Recovery built in:** Breaks, meals, exercise, social time—not afterthoughts
- **Batching:** All admin/email in one 1-hour block (not scattered throughout day)
- **Realistic:** Only ~4 hours of deep work (not 8-10), plenty of buffer

**Your Turn:**
1. Copy template or create your own time-blocking schedule
2. Use YOUR energy audit from Activity 1 to match tasks to energy levels
3. Schedule 1-2 deep work blocks during YOUR peak mental energy
4. Include all recovery activities (breaks, meals, exercise, social, sleep)
5. Calculate: What % of waking hours is Q2?
   - Goal: 40-50% Q2 (up from typical 5-10%)

**Common Mistakes to Avoid:**
- **Mistake 1:** Scheduling 8+ hours of deep work (impossible, leads to failure)
  - Fix: 2-4 hours deep work is realistic maximum
- **Mistake 2:** No buffer time (schedule falls apart with one unexpected task)
  - Fix: Plan for only 60-70% of day
- **Mistake 3:** Fighting your energy patterns (scheduling hard cognitive work during low energy)
  - Fix: Use YOUR energy audit, not generic advice
- **Mistake 4:** No recovery scheduled (burnout within weeks)
  - Fix: Breaks, exercise, social time are non-negotiable

**Your Task:**
Identify how you'll test this time-blocking approach this week.

**Questions:**

- Partner asks: "What's most different from your current schedule?"

**Key Takeaway:**
Time-blocking forces realistic assessment of what you can actually accomplish in a day. Most students over-schedule (optimistic planning fallacy). A good time-block leaves you feeling accomplished, not overwhelmed.

## Activity 3: Habit Stacking Design (25 minutes)

**Assessment Connection:** Your PDP goals require behavior change—learning new skills, building professional networks, maintaining health while pursuing ambitious targets. Willpower alone fails. Habit stacks automate these behaviors, making goal progress effortless. You'll reference these habit systems in your PDP implementation section.

**The Challenge:**
You'll design habit stacks using Clear's Four Laws, creating automated routines that support your Personal Development Plan goals.

### Part 1: Identify Target Habits (7 min)

**Your Task:**
Choose 2-3 habits that would significantly advance your PDP goals if done consistently.

**Habit Categories Aligned with PDP:**

**Skill Development Habits:**
- Daily language practice (15 min)

- Daily reading in your field
- Daily writing practice

**Career Preparation Habits:**
- Weekly informational interview outreach
- Daily LinkedIn engagement

**Reflection/Learning Habits:**

**Selection Criteria:**
1. **Small enough to do daily:** If too big, you won't sustain it
   - Good: "Read 10 pages in field-related book"

2. **Directly supports PDP goal:** Clear connection to 1-year, 3-year, or 5-year plan

**Your Turn:**

2. List 3-5 habits that would support these goals if done consistently

**Example:**
- **Goal:** Improve business English communication skills for multinational career
- **Habit 1:** Daily 15-minute English podcast listening
  - Impact: Vocabulary expansion, pronunciation modeling, business context exposure
- **Habit 2:** Daily 10-minute writing in English journal
  - Impact: Writing fluency, grammar practice, reflection habit
- **Habit 3:** Weekly submission of English writing to tutor/mentor for feedback
  - Impact: Error correction, improvement tracking

### Part 2: Apply Four Laws to Each Habit (10 min)

**Your Task:**
Design each habit using Clear's Four Laws to maximize success probability.

**The Four Laws Template:**

**Habit:**
*[State your specific habit]*

**1st Law: Make It Obvious (Cue Design)**
- **Implementation intention:** "I will [BEHAVIOR] at [TIME] in [LOCATION]"
- **Habit stack:** "After [CURRENT HABIT], I will [NEW HABIT]"
- **Environment design:** What visual cue will trigger this?

**2nd Law: Make It Attractive (Craving Design)**
- **Temptation bundling:** Can you pair this habit with something you enjoy?
- **Social motivation:** Can you do this with others or share progress?

**3rd Law: Make It Easy (Response Design)**
- **Two-Minute Rule:** What's the minimal version that starts the behavior?
- **Reduce friction:** What prep makes this habit easier to start?

**4th Law: Make It Satisfying (Reward Design)**
- **Immediate reward:** What feels good right after completing?
- **Habit tracking:** How will you track consistency?

**Example Application:**

**Habit:** Daily 15-minute English podcast listening

**1st Law: Make It Obvious**
- Implementation intention: "I will listen to English podcast at 7:30am in kitchen"
- Habit stack: "After I pour my morning coffee, I will start podcast"
- Environment design: Earbuds on kitchen counter next to coffee maker

**2nd Law: Make It Attractive**
- Temptation bundling: "I only drink my favorite coffee while listening to podcast" (coffee = reward for podcast)
- Social motivation: Share interesting insights with study group weekly

**3rd Law: Make It Easy**
- Two-Minute Rule: "Just start the episode"—don't commit to finishing; often will continue
- Reduce friction: Podcast app open to queued episode night before; earbuds charged and ready

**4th Law: Make It Satisfying**
- Immediate reward: Coffee enjoyment paired with podcast; feeling of "learned something"
- Habit tracking: Check box on calendar; aim for 30-day streak

**Your Turn:**
1. Take your 2-3 selected habits
2. For each, fill out the Four Laws template
3. Be specific—vague plans fail
4. Focus especially on 1st Law (Obvious)—this is where most habits fail

**Common Mistakes:**

**Mistake 1: Vague implementation intention**
- Bad: "I will practice English when I have time"
- Good: "I will practice English at 8pm in library"

**Mistake 2: Habit too big to be "easy"**
- Bad: "I will exercise for 60 minutes daily"
- Good: "I will do 10 push-ups after brushing teeth" (can extend if motivated, but 10 is minimum)

**Mistake 3: No immediate reward**
- Bad: "The reward is getting better over time" (too delayed)
- Good: "I track my streak and celebrate each week completed"

### Part 3: Build a Habit Stack Chain (8 min)

**Your Task:**
Combine multiple habits into one automatic sequence using the habit stacking formula.

**The Formula:**
> "After [CURRENT HABIT], I will [NEW HABIT #1]. After [NEW HABIT #1], I will [NEW HABIT #2]."

**Why Chains Work:**
- First habit in chain triggers second
- Second triggers third
- Entire routine becomes one automatic behavior
- Especially powerful for morning/evening routines

**Example Morning Routine Habit Stack:**

```
After my alarm goes off, I will sit up immediately (no snooze).
After I sit up, I will drink the water on my nightstand.
After I drink water, I will make my bed.
After I make my bed, I will do 10 push-ups.
After push-ups, I will shower.
After shower, I will review my time-block schedule while eating breakfast.
```

**Result:** Six habits stacked into one automatic 30-minute morning routine. After 3-4 weeks, alarm → entire routine (no decisions required).

**Example Evening Study Routine Habit Stack:**

```
After I sit down at my desk, I will put my phone in the drawer.
After I put phone in drawer, I will review my deep work goal for this session (what will I accomplish?).
After I set my goal, I will set a 50-minute timer.
After the timer goes off, I will take a 10-minute walk break.
After my walk, I will do one more 50-minute deep work session.
After my second session, I will write 3 sentences about what I learned.
```

**Result:** Phone removal, goal-setting, timed work, breaks, and reflection all become automatic.

**Your Turn:**
1. Choose one routine where you want to build habits: morning, evening study, pre-bed, post-class
2. Identify existing reliable anchor (alarm, sitting at desk, finishing dinner, etc.)
3. Stack 3-5 new habits onto this anchor using formula
4. Write out complete chain: "After X, I will Y. After Y, I will Z..."
5. Identify: What's the first habit that might fail? How can you make that one easier?

**Design Principles:**
- **Anchor to reliable existing habit:** Something you already do daily automatically
- **Sequence logically:** Habits should flow naturally (don't stack unrelated behaviors)
- **Start small:** 3 habits in chain, not 10 (can expand later)
- **Energy-appropriate:** Don't stack mentally demanding habits when energy is low

**Pair Check-In (3 min per person):**
- Share your habit stack chain with partner
- Partner evaluates: "Is the anchor reliable? Do habits flow logically? Are they small enough?"
- Partner asks: "Which habit in the chain is most likely to fail? How can you make it easier?"

**Key Takeaway:**
Habit stacks reduce decision fatigue. Instead of deciding "Should I exercise today?" it becomes automatic: "I always exercise after dinner." Your limited willpower is preserved for truly difficult decisions.

**Daily Recoveries:**
- Morning ritual: 10-min journaling about yesterday's wins (not what's undone)
- Evening ritual: 30-min activity purely for joy (music, art, cooking, gaming—no productivity goal)
- Why these restore emotional energy: Starting day with positive focus; ending with pleasure/play

**Weekly Recoveries:**
- Weekend ritual: 3-hour block with friends (meal, activity, genuine connection)
- Social/hobby ritual: Sunday painting class (creative expression, community)
- Why these restore emotional energy: Deep social connection; creative outlet for emotions

**Semester Recoveries:**
- Between exams: One full afternoon doing something fun (not academic)
- Between semesters: Week focused on relationships and hobbies, minimal alone time
- Why these restore emotional energy: Extended immersion in emotionally nourishing activities

**Design Principles:**
- **Scheduled, not aspirational:** These go in your time-blocks
- **Non-negotiable:** Recovery isn't "if I have time"—it's protected
- **Targeted:** Match recovery to your bottleneck dimension
- **Realistic:** If ritual requires 2 hours and you never have 2 hours, it won't happen

## Key Takeaways

**What You Learned Today:**

1. **Energy management trumps time management:** Your productivity is limited by renewable energy (physical, emotional, mental, spiritual), not by hours in the day. Strategic recovery expands capacity.

2. **Quadrant 2 is where success lives:** Important but Not Urgent activities (skill-building, relationship-building, health, planning) prevent crises and enable long-term goal achievement. Protect Q2 time.

3. **Time-blocking matches energy to tasks:** Schedule demanding work during peak energy; schedule recovery during low energy. Plans that fight your biology fail.

4. **Habits take ~66 days and follow Four Laws:** Make good habits Obvious, Attractive, Easy, Satisfying. Break bad habits by inverting these laws. Habit stacks automate behavior.

5. **Recovery enables performance:** High performance requires oscillation between stress and renewal. Scheduled recovery (micro, daily, weekly, semester) is non-negotiable for sustainable achievement.

6. **Systems beat willpower:** Willpower is finite and depletes. Time-blocks, habit stacks, and recovery rituals automate success without relying on daily motivation.
