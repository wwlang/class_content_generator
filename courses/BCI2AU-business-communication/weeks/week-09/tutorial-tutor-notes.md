## Valid Student Approaches

Students may organize their presentations differently. Do NOT impose one "right" structure. Valid variations include:

### Approach 1: Sequential Topic Progression
- **Structure:** Member A covers Challenge 1, Member B covers Challenge 2, Member C covers Challenge 3, Member D synthesizes solutions
- **Transitions:** "Now that we've seen the power distance challenge, [Name] will explain how uncertainty avoidance creates different issues..."
- **Strength:** Clear logical flow; easy to balance time
- **Watch for:** Can feel disconnected if transitions aren't explicit

### Approach 2: Framework-Based Division
- **Structure:** Member A introduces all challenges, Member B applies Hofstede analysis, Member C applies Meyer's feedback framework, Member D concludes with psychological safety recommendations
- **Transitions:** "Building on [Name's] cultural analysis, I'll show how Meyer's Three A's help navigate these differences..."
- **Strength:** Shows sophisticated framework integration
- **Watch for:** Requires strong conceptual connections between speakers

### Approach 3: Case Study Narrative
- **Structure:** Team tells story of a Vietnamese manager encountering each cultural challenge sequentially, with each member advancing the narrative
- **Transitions:** "[Name] showed how the first meeting went wrong. Let me show what happened next when..."
- **Strength:** Engaging and cohesive
- **Watch for:** Harder to balance speaking time if narrative is uneven

### Approach 4: Problem-Solution Pairs
- **Structure:** Pairs of speakersâ€”one identifies problem, partner presents solution; rotate through 3 problem-solution sets
- **Transitions:** "As [Name] explained, this creates confusion. Here's how we solve it..."
- **Strength:** Built-in coordination between pairs
- **Watch for:** Need to ensure both members of pair have equal time

**Tutor Role:** Validate all approaches. Focus feedback on **execution of transitions and balance**, not the structural choice itself.

## Common Misconceptions to Address

### Misconception 1: "Transitions mean just saying 'next topic' or 'next speaker'"

**Reality:** Excellent transitions **build on previous content** and **preview next content**.

**Bad transition:** "That's all from me. Next is [Name]." [awkward pause]

**Good transition:** "As I've shown, power distance creates hierarchy challenges. Building on this, [Name] will demonstrate how this plays out in daily meeting dynamics."

**Correction:** "Your transition is a bridge connecting two ideas, not just a handoff. Use the previous speaker's key word and your own preview."

### Misconception 2: "Equal participation means everyone introduces themselves"

**Reality:** Equal participation means **equal substantive content**, not just equal number of sentences.

**Bad balance:** Member A: 2 min of analysis; Member B: 2 min of analysis; Member C: 30 sec conclusion; Member D: 30 sec introduction

**Good balance:** 4 members Ã— 52 seconds each = 3.5 min core content + shared intro/conclusion

**Correction:** "Everyone needs roughly equal time presenting core contentâ€”not just speaking a few words."

### Misconception 3: "Looking unified means we all dress the same or stand in a line"

**Reality:** Unity comes from **message coordination and mutual support**, not physical uniformity.

**Indicators of unity:**
- Speakers reference each other by name
- Eye contact between members while speaking
- Nodding/active listening when not speaking
- Building on previous points explicitly
- Supporting each other during Q&A

**Correction:** "Unity is about how you connect verbally and non-verbally, not about matching outfits."

### Misconception 4: "This is just practice, so quality doesn't matter"

**Reality:** Practice quality predicts Week 12 performance. Teams that coordinate well today will coordinate well under pressure.

**The stakes:** This practice represents 40% of course grade (25% Group + 15% Individual).

**Correction:** "Treat this like a dress rehearsalâ€”your habits formed today become your Week 12 performance. If you fumble transitions now, you'll fumble them in the graded presentation."

### Misconception 5: "Cultural awareness only matters if we have international students"

**Reality:** Cultural frameworks apply to ALL team diversityâ€”generational, gender, regional, disciplinary.

**Vietnamese context diversity:**
- Northern vs. Southern Vietnamese communication styles differ
- Generation gaps (older students often more hierarchical)
- Gender dynamics (Vietnamese PDI intersects with gender roles)
- Academic backgrounds (business vs. engineering communication norms)

**Correction:** "Even in all-Vietnamese teams, use these frameworks to navigate differences. Plus, 89% of professionals work on global virtual teamsâ€”you WILL need these skills."

## Vietnamese Cultural Considerations

### High Power Distance (PDI 70) Impact on Tutorial

**Challenge 1: Senior/Older Students Dominating Speaking Time**

**What you'll see:** In mixed-age teams, older students speak for 2 minutes, younger students speak for 20 seconds.

**Why:** Vietnamese PDI = deference to seniority and age

**Intervention:**

"Your speaking time is 90 seconds for [senior student], 30 seconds for [junior student]. That violates equal participation. For Week 12 grading, this costs you points. Rebalance to 60 seconds each. [Junior student], I want to hear you present a full section, not just introduce the team."

**Cultural bridge:** "In Western business presentations, junior employees often present research findings while senior employees field Q&A. Everyone contributes substantively. Practice that norm today."

### Collectivism (IDV 20) Impact on Tutorial

**Challenge 2: Harmony Over Candor in Feedback**

**What you'll see:** Phase 5 debrief consists of "Good job! We did great! No problems!"

**Why:** Vietnamese collectivism = group harmony valued over individual opinions

**Intervention:**

"If you tell each other everything was perfect, you'll make the same mistakes in Week 12. Real teamwork means honest feedback. Everyone must identify ONE thing to improve. I'll start: [Name], your transition at 1:30 had a 4-second gap. That's fixableâ€”write out your transition sentence. Now you give one suggestion to a teammate."

**Cultural bridge:** "In Learning Zone (Edmondson's framework), high accountability requires honest feedback. You can maintain relationship AND give constructive criticism using Meyer's Three A's."

### Indirect Communication (Meyer's Evaluating Dimension)

**Challenge 3: Vague, Face-Saving Feedback**

**What you'll see:** "Maybe we could perhaps consider possibly making the transitions a little bit smoother..." (downgraders piled on)

**Why:** Vietnamese culture = indirect feedback to preserve face

**Intervention:**

"That's too indirect for your team to act on. Be more explicit: 'Our transition from section 1 to section 2 needs work. Let's write out the exact sentence [Name] will say to hand off to [Name].' You can be direct AND respectful."

**Cultural bridge:** "When giving feedback to international teammates or Western managers, increase your directness by 30%. Practice now."

### Communication Apprehension Impact

**Challenge 4: Silent Members During Presentations**

**What you'll see:** One confident member presents 4 minutes, three shy members present 20 seconds total

**Why:** Vietnamese students often have high communication apprehension + face-saving concerns

**Intervention (Private with Team):**

"[Quiet member], you need to present substantive content for Week 12's Individual Grade. Your team's job is to support you. [To team]: How can you make [quiet member] feel safe? Set the Stage (Edmondson)â€”tell them their input is essential, not optional."

**Coaching Quiet Member:**

"You know the content. Practice your 60-second section with me right now. [Listen.] Good. Do exactly that in the presentation. Your team will support you."
