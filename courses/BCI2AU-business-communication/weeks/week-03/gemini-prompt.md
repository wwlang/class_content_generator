<slide number="6" layout="section-break" title="SEGMENT 1: The Pyramid Principle">

# SEGMENT 1
## The Pyramid Principle

**The framework used by McKinsey, BCG, Bain, and every M7 business school**

Developed by Barbara Minto at McKinsey (1963-1973)

</slide>

<slide number="7" layout="framework" title="The Pyramid Structure">

## The Pyramid Structure

```
                 MAIN ANSWER
                /     |      \
           Arg 1    Arg 2    Arg 3
          /  |  \  /  |  \  /  |  \
       Data Data Data Data Data Data
```

**Three Rules:**
1. Ideas above must summarize those below
2. Grouped ideas must be logically the same kind
3. Ideas must follow logical order (chronological, structural, comparative, or deductive)

</slide>

<slide number="8" layout="comparison" title="Think Bottom-Up, Present Top-Down">

## Think Bottom-Up, Present Top-Down

| **How You Think (Analysis)** | **How You Present (Document)** |
|-------------------------------|--------------------------------|
| 1. Gather data | 1. State recommendation |
| 2. Identify patterns | 2. Provide 3 supporting arguments |
| 3. Form arguments | 3. Share key data as needed |
| 4. Reach conclusion | 4. Offer next steps |

**The magic: You do the hard thinking so your reader doesn't have to.**

</slide>

<slide number="9" layout="framework" title="Case Study: McKinsey Recommendation">

## Case Study: McKinsey Recommendation

**Client Question:** Should we enter the Vietnamese electric vehicle market?

**Pyramid Structure:**

**Recommendation (Top):**
Yes, enter now with a $50M investment targeting premium urban buyers in Hanoi and HCMC.

**Supporting Arguments (Level 2):**
1. **Market timing:** EV adoption growing 40% year-over-year in major cities
2. **Competitive gap:** Only 2 established players, gap in premium segment
3. **Financial return:** Projected 3-year breakeven with 25% gross margin

**Data (Level 3):** Market research, competitor analysis, financial models

</slide>

<slide number="10" layout="framework" title="The MECE Principle">

## The MECE Principle

**MECE = Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive**

- **Mutually Exclusive:** No overlap between ideas
- **Collectively Exhaustive:** Nothing relevant is missing

**Bad Example (Violates MECE):**
1. Increase revenue
2. Expand into new markets ← *Overlaps with #1*
3. Improve customer retention

**Good Example (MECE):**
1. Acquire new customers
2. Increase revenue per customer
3. Improve customer retention

</slide>

<slide number="11" layout="content" title="Logical Ordering Methods">

## Logical Ordering Methods

**Rule 3: Ideas must follow logical order**

Four ordering methods for your supporting arguments:

1. **Chronological:** Past → Present → Future
   *Example: "We missed Q1 targets, Q2 is tracking below, Q3 requires intervention"*

2. **Structural:** By division, department, or geography
   *Example: "North region is underperforming, Central is meeting targets, South is exceeding"*

3. **Comparative:** By importance, priority, or magnitude
   *Example: "Cost reduction is critical, revenue growth is important, brand awareness is beneficial"*

4. **Deductive:** If A and B, then C
   *Example: "Costs are rising 10% annually, revenue is flat, therefore we must cut expenses"*

</slide>

<slide number="12" layout="content" title="Pyramid Principle: Common Mistakes">

## Pyramid Principle: Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Burying the Lead**
❌ "After analyzing data from 15 sources over 3 months..."
✅ "We should approve the project. Here's why..."

**Mistake 2: Non-MECE Arguments**
❌ Arguments: Cost savings, Efficiency gains, Reduced waste *(all overlap)*
✅ Arguments: Lower costs, Faster delivery, Higher quality *(MECE)*

**Mistake 3: Mixing Logical Levels**
❌ "Hire 3 engineers" + "Improve product quality" *(tactic + goal)*
✅ "Hire 3 engineers" + "Upgrade testing tools" + "Implement code review" *(all tactics)*

</slide>

<slide number="13" layout="section-break" title="SEGMENT 2: SCQA Framework">

# SEGMENT 2
## SCQA Framework

**How to write document introductions that hook your reader**

**S**ituation → **C**omplication → **Q**uestion → **A**nswer

</slide>

<slide number="14" layout="framework" title="SCQA Breakdown">

## SCQA Breakdown

**S — Situation:** Indisputable context the audience already accepts
> *"Our customer retention rate has held steady at 85% for 3 years."*

**C — Complication:** The problem or change creating urgency
> *"However, our top competitor just launched a loyalty program, and we're seeing early churn signals in the premium segment."*

**Q — Question:** What naturally arises from the complication
> *"How should we respond to protect our customer base?"*

**A — Answer:** Your recommendation (the pyramid top)
> *"We should launch a premium-tier loyalty program by Q2, focusing on our top 20% of customers."*

</slide>

<slide number="15" layout="content" title="SCQA in Action: Full Email Opening">

## SCQA in Action: Full Email Opening

**To:** Regional Director
**Subject:** Premium Loyalty Program Recommendation

**Email Opening:**

Our customer retention rate has held steady at 85% for three years, contributing to predictable revenue growth. **[S]** However, our top competitor launched a points-based loyalty program last month, and we're seeing early churn signals among premium customers—15% increase in support tickets asking about loyalty benefits. **[C]** How should we respond to protect our most valuable customer segment? **[Q]**

We should launch a premium-tier loyalty program by Q2, focusing on the top 20% of customers who generate 60% of our revenue. **[A]** This approach will require $200K investment but is projected to reduce churn by 5 percentage points, protecting $2M in annual revenue.

**The next paragraph would present your 3 supporting arguments...**

</slide>

<slide number="16" layout="comparison" title="SCQA Variations by Context">

## SCQA Variations by Context

| **Context** | **Situation** | **Complication** |
|-------------|---------------|------------------|
| **Proposal** | Current state | Gap or opportunity |
| **Status Update** | Project milestone | Blocker or risk |
| **Recommendation** | Industry trend | Competitive threat |
| **Request** | Team capacity | Resource constraint |

**The pattern stays the same; the content adapts.**

</slide>

<slide number="17" layout="content" title="Writing Strong Complications">

## Writing Strong Complications

**The Complication creates urgency. Make it concrete and specific.**

**Weak Complications (vague, abstract):**
- "There might be some issues with the current approach"
- "Competition is increasing"
- "We should improve customer satisfaction"

**Strong Complications (specific, urgent):**
- "Current approach created $47K in errors last quarter—12% of budget"
- "Three competitors entered our market in Q1; we've lost 8% market share"
- "Customer satisfaction dropped from 4.2 to 3.6 stars; NPS fell 15 points"

**Formula: Specific problem + Quantified impact + Time urgency**

</slide>

# SEGMENT 3
## Clarity & Concision

**Making every word count**

Based on *The Elements of Style* by Strunk & White
(#1 most-assigned text in US higher education)

</slide>

<slide number="19" layout="quote" title="Rule 1: Omit Needless Words">

> "Vigorous writing is concise. A sentence should contain no unnecessary words, a paragraph no unnecessary sentences, for the same reason that a drawing should have no unnecessary lines and a machine no unnecessary parts."
>
> — William Strunk Jr.

**Translation: Make every word earn its place.**

</slide>

<slide number="20" layout="comparison" title="Omit Needless Words: Examples">

## Omit Needless Words: Examples

| **Before (Wordy)** | **After (Concise)** | **Words Saved** |
|--------------------|---------------------|-----------------|
| In order to improve performance | To improve performance | 2 words |
| Due to the fact that | Because | 4 words |
| At this point in time | Now | 3 words |
| In the event that | If | 3 words |
| It is important to note that | [Delete entirely] | 6 words |
| In my opinion, I believe that | [Delete entirely] | 5 words |

**In a 500-word memo, eliminating these saves 50+ words (10% reduction).**

</slide>

<slide number="21" layout="content" title="More Phrases to Eliminate">

## More Phrases to Eliminate

**Common padding phrases that add zero value:**

| **Delete This** | **Say This Instead** |
|-----------------|----------------------|
| For the purpose of | To, For |
| In regard to | About |
| With reference to | About |
| Prior to | Before |
| Subsequent to | After |
| In close proximity to | Near |
| Exhibits a tendency to | Often, Tends to |
| Has the ability to | Can |
| Is able to | Can |
| In spite of the fact that | Although, Despite |

**These bloat your writing without adding meaning or formality.**

</slide>

<slide number="22" layout="framework" title="Rule 2: Use Active Voice">

## Rule 2: Use Active Voice

**Passive Voice (Weak):**
"The report was completed by the team."
*Subject receives action*

**Active Voice (Strong):**
"The team completed the report."
*Subject performs action*

**Why active voice?**
- Clearer responsibility and accountability
- Shorter, more direct sentences
- More confident, assertive tone

</slide>

<slide number="23" layout="content" title="Active Voice: More Examples">

## Active Voice: More Examples

**Transform these passive constructions to active:**

| **Passive (Weak)** | **Active (Strong)** |
|--------------------|---------------------|
| The proposal was rejected by management | Management rejected the proposal |
| Errors were found in the data | We found errors in the data |
| A decision will be made by Friday | We will decide by Friday |
| The policy was implemented to reduce costs | We implemented the policy to reduce costs |
| It is believed that sales will increase | We believe sales will increase |
| It was determined that... | We determined that... |

**Notice: Active voice is shorter AND clearer.**

</slide>

<slide number="24" layout="comparison" title="Rule 3: Use Definite, Specific, Concrete Language">

## Rule 3: Use Definite, Specific, Concrete Language

| **Vague** | **Specific** |
|-----------|--------------|
| Sales increased significantly | Sales increased 23% quarter-over-quarter |
| Many customers complained | 47 customers filed complaints (15% of total base) |
| We need to improve soon | We need to implement changes by March 15 |
| The project is going well | The project is 85% complete, on budget, 3 days ahead of schedule |

**Specificity builds credibility.**

</slide>

<slide number="25" layout="content" title="Replace Vague Words with Specifics">

## Replace Vague Words with Specifics

**Common vague words to avoid:**

| **Vague Word** | **Replace With** |
|----------------|------------------|
| Soon | By March 15, Within 2 weeks, Q2 2025 |
| Recently | Last Tuesday, In November, Q4 2024 |
| Many | 127, 34%, Two-thirds |
| Few | 3, 12%, Less than 5% |
| Large | $2.4M, 340 units, 15,000 sq ft |
| Significant | 23%, $450K, 3.2x increase |
| Improve | Increase by 15%, Reduce errors to <2%, Achieve 4.5 stars |
| Several | 5, 12, Between 8-10 |

**Default to numbers whenever possible.**

</slide>

## Editing Exercise: Before Version

**Memo Draft (First Version):**

*"In order to improve customer satisfaction, it is recommended that a new feedback system should be implemented. Due to the fact that many customers have expressed concerns about response times, it is important that we address this issue soon. The new system will allow customers to rate their experience, and improvements will be made based on the feedback that is received."*

**Word count:** 65 words
**Problems:** Passive voice, needless words, vague language

**What would you cut or change?**

</slide>

<slide number="27" layout="content" title="Editing Exercise: After Version">

## Editing Exercise: After Version

**Memo Draft (Edited Version):**

*"To improve customer satisfaction, we recommend implementing a new feedback system by March 31. Over 120 customers (38% of Q4 survey respondents) reported slow response times. The new system will allow customers to rate their experience on a 5-point scale, and we will address issues scoring below 3.0 within 48 hours."*

**Word count:** 54 words (17% shorter)
**Improvements:** Active voice, specific data, clear deadlines

</slide>

<slide number="28" layout="framework" title="Purpose-Person-Point Framework">

## Purpose-Person-Point Framework

**Before you write anything, answer 3 questions:**

1. **Purpose:** What result do I want?
   *(Inform? Request? Persuade?)*

2. **Person:** Who is my reader?
   *(What do they need to understand my message?)*

3. **Point:** What's my essential message?
   *(What must they know or do?)*

**Clear thinking → Clear writing**

*Source: Danziger, E. (2024). How to take your business writing from "average" to "great." Harvard Business Review.*

</slide>

<slide number="29" layout="framework" title="What/So What/Now What Framework">

## What/So What/Now What Framework

**A simple structure for any business communication:**

- **What:** Describe the facts, situation, or product
  > *"Our Q1 sales increased 15% to $2.3M"*

- **So What:** Explain the relevance to your audience
  > *"This puts us on track to exceed our annual target by 10%"*

- **Now What:** Outline next steps or call-to-action
  > *"Let's allocate the surplus to hire two sales reps in Q2"*

**Use this for emails, updates, presentations, meetings.**

*Source: Abrahams, M. (2024). A simple hack to help you communicate more effectively. Harvard Business Review.*

</slide>

<slide number="30" layout="content" title="Executive Summary Best Practices">

## Executive Summary Best Practices

**The ultimate test of top-down thinking:**

1. **Lead with your recommendation** (first sentence)
2. **State the impact** (financial, strategic, operational)
3. **Provide 3 supporting arguments** (Pyramid structure)
4. **End with next steps** (clear call to action)

**Length: 1 page maximum (250-300 words)**

**Your Week 10 assessment will test this skill.**

</slide>

<slide number="31" layout="framework" title="How Frameworks Connect">

## How Frameworks Connect

**Your Writing Process (Week 4 Memo):**

1. **Clarify thinking:** Purpose-Person-Point
   *What am I trying to achieve?*

2. **Structure argument:** Pyramid Principle
   *Main answer + 3 supporting arguments*

3. **Craft opening:** SCQA
   *Situation-Complication-Question-Answer*

4. **Edit sentences:** Strunk & White rules
   *Omit needless words, active voice, be specific*

**Each framework serves a different stage of writing.**

</slide>

## Common Writing Scenarios

**Apply these frameworks to real business situations:**

| **Scenario** | **Framework to Use** | **Key Focus** |
|--------------|---------------------|---------------|
| **Cold email to potential client** | SCQA + Purpose-Person-Point | Hook them fast, show relevance |
| **Weekly status report to manager** | What/So What/Now What | Facts, impact, next steps |
| **Budget approval request** | Pyramid + SCQA | Recommendation first, justify with data |

| **Problem escalation** | SCQA with strong Complication | Create urgency, provide solution |
